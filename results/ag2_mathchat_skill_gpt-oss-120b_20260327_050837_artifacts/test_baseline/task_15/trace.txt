**Explanation**

Let  

\[
N = 25a + r ,\qquad 0\le r < 25,
\]

where \(a=\lfloor N/25\rfloor\) is the number of 25‑cent coins taken by the greedy algorithm and \(r\) is the remainder.

The greedy algorithm uses  

\[
\text{coins}_{\text{greedy}} = a + \Big\lfloor\frac{r}{10}\Big\rfloor + (r\bmod 10)
\]

coins.

If we replace one 25‑cent coin by three 10‑cent coins, we must make the amount \(25+r\) with 10‑cent and 1‑cent coins.  
Let  

\[
t = \Big\lfloor\frac{25+r}{10}\Big\rfloor,\qquad u = (25+r)\bmod 10 .
\]

Then the alternative uses  

\[
\text{coins}_{\text{alt}} = (a-1) + t + u .
\]

The greedy method fails exactly when  

\[
\text{coins}_{\text{greedy}} > \text{coins}_{\text{alt}} .
\]

Computing the difference  

\[
\Delta = 1 + \Big\lfloor\frac{r}{10}\Big\rfloor + (r\bmod 10) - t - u
\]

for each possible remainder \(r\) (0–24) yields  

\[
\Delta>0 \Longleftrightarrow r\in\{5,6,7,8,9,15,16,17,18,19\}.
\]

Thus the greedy algorithm is **not optimal** precisely when the remainder after removing all possible 25‑cent coins is 5–9 or 15–19.

A failure can only happen when at least one 25‑cent coin is used, i.e. when \(N\ge 25\).

In each block of 25 consecutive integers there are 10 residues that cause failure.  
From 1 to 1000 there are  

\[
\left\lfloor\frac{1000}{25}\right\rfloor = 40
\]

full blocks, giving \(40\times 10 = 400\) numbers.

The first block (1–24) contains the numbers \(5,6,7,8,9,15,16,17,18,19\); for these \(N<25\) the greedy algorithm uses only 1‑cent and 10‑cent coins, and it is optimal.  
Hence we subtract these 10 numbers:

\[
\text{failures}=400-10 = 390 .
\]

The total number of integers \(N\) with \(1\le N\le 1000\) is 1000, so the number for which the greedy algorithm **succeeds** is

\[
1000 - 390 = 610 .
\]
