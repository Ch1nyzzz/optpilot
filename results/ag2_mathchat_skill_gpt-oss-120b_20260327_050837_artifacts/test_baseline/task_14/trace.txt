For a rectangle whose vertices are taken from the vertices of a regular \(12\)-gon, each side of the rectangle is a chord of the circum‑circle (a side or a diagonal of the polygon).  
In a circle an inscribed angle is a right angle **iff** it subtends a diameter.  
Hence every angle of an inscribed rectangle must subtend a diameter, which forces the two opposite vertices of the rectangle to be *antipodal* points on the circle.

Therefore a rectangle inside the regular \(12\)-gon is completely determined by **two distinct diameters** (pairs of opposite vertices).  
Conversely, any two distinct diameters intersect at the centre of the circle and their four endpoints form a rectangle (a square when the diameters are perpendicular).

So the problem reduces to counting unordered pairs of distinct diameters among the vertices of the regular \(12\)-gon.

---

### Counting the diameters

- The regular \(12\)-gon has \(12\) vertices.
- Opposite vertices are paired, giving exactly \(\dfrac{12}{2}=6\) diameters.

---

### Counting unordered pairs of diameters

From the \(6\) diameters we choose any two different ones:

\[
\binom{6}{2}= \frac{6\cdot5}{2}=15 .
\]

Each such pair yields a unique rectangle, and no rectangle is counted twice because the two diameters uniquely determine its four vertices.
