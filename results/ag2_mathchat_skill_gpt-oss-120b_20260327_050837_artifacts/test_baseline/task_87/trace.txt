### 1.  An upper bound

Count the ordered pairs \((x,y)\) with \(x\neq y\) in two ways.

*For a fixed point \(x\)* there are at most \(k\) different values of
\(D(x,y)\); each value can appear for arbitrarily many \(y\)’s, but
the **number of distinct values** contributed by \(x\) to the whole
collection of ordered pairs is at most \(k\).

Hence the total number of distinct *scale‑values* that appear among all
ordered pairs is at most  

\[
\sum_{x\in\mathcal F}|S(x)|\le k|\mathcal F|.
\tag{1}
\]

*For a fixed unordered pair \(\{x,y\}\)* the distance \(|x-y|\) has a
single dyadic scale \(d\). This same scale is counted **twice** in the
sum (once for the ordered pair \((x,y)\) and once for \((y,x)\)).
Consequently each unordered pair contributes **exactly two** to the
left‑hand side of (1).

If \(|\mathcal F|=n\) then there are \(\binom{n}{2}=n(n-1)/2\) unordered
pairs, so (1) gives  

\[
2\binom{n}{2}=n(n-1)\le k n\qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad n-1\le 2k .
\]

Thus  

\[
\boxed{\,|\mathcal F|\le 2k+1\,}.
\tag{2}
\]
