### 1.  Why we cannot do it with fewer than \(n\) marks  

Divide the \(2n\times 2n\) board into the \(n^{2}\) disjoint \(2\times 2\) squares  

\[
\begin{array}{|c|c|c|c|c|}
\hline
\multicolumn{2}{|c|}{\square_{1,1}} &
\multicolumn{2}{c|}{\square_{1,2}} & \dots  \\ \hline
\multicolumn{2}{|c|}{\square_{2,1}} &
\multicolumn{2}{c|}{\square_{2,2}} & \dots  \\ \hline
\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots &      \\ \hline
\end{array}
\]

Every domino that lies completely inside one of these little squares is either

* two vertical dominoes, or  
* two horizontal dominoes.

Hence a \(2\times 2\) square that contains **no** marked cell can be tiled in two
different ways while all the other squares are tiled arbitrarily.
Consequently, if the set of marked cells contains fewer than one cell in each
row of squares, i.e. if  

\[
k < n,
\]

then at least one of the \(2\times 2\) squares is completely unmarked, and the
board admits at least two different domino‑tilings that avoid a double‑marked
domino.  
Therefore a unique tiling is impossible when \(k<n\).

--------------------------------------------------------------------

### 2.  A construction with exactly \(n\) marks  

We now show that \(k=n\) **can** be achieved.  
Number the rows and columns from \(1\) to \(2n\).  
Mark the cells  

\[
(1,1),\;(2,3),\;(3,5),\dots ,\;(n,2n-1).
\tag{1}
\]

These are the cells that lie on the “main diagonal’’ of the board, but we
skip one column after each step; the picture for \(n=4\) is

```
M . . . . . . .
. . M . . . . .
. . . . M . . .
. . . . . . M .
. . . . . . . .
. . . . . . . .
. . . . . . . .
. . . . . . . .
```
(\(M\) = marked).

Compute minimal k.**Solution Overview**

For a \(2n\times 2n\) board we consider the grid graph \(G_{2n,2n}\) whose vertices are the cells and whose edges join orthogonal neighbours (the possible positions of a domino).  
A *tiling* of the board is a perfect matching of this graph.

A set \(M\) of cells is **admissible** for a tiling \(T\) iff no domino of \(T\) contains two cells of \(M\).  
We are asked for the smallest integer \(k\) for which there exists a set \(M\) of \(k\) cells such that **exactly one** tiling of the board is admissible for \(M\).

Thus we have to find the minimum size of a *forcing set* (in the terminology of perfect‑matching theory) that does **not** contain both ends of any forced edge.

The problem can be tackled experimentally for small \(n\) by enumerating all tilings and testing all subsets of cells.  
The results suggest a simple closed formula, which we then prove.
