We need the maximum possible value of  

\[
F(\mathbf x)=\sum_{1\le r<s\le 2n}(s-r-n)\,x_r x_s ,
\qquad -1\le x_i\le 1 .
\]

The coefficients depend only on the distance \(d=s-r\):
\[
c_{d}=d-n\qquad (d=1,2,\dots ,2n-1).
\]

Thus  

\[
F(\mathbf x)=\sum_{d=1}^{2n-1}(d-n)\sum_{r=1}^{2n-d}x_r x_{r+d}.
\tag{1}
\]

The problem is a quadratic optimisation with a symmetric matrix  
\(A=(a_{rs})\) where  

\[
a_{rs}= \begin{cases}
\frac12(s-r-n), & r<s,\\[2pt]
\frac12(r-s-n), & r>s,\\[2pt]
0, & r=s .
\end{cases}
\]

Because the feasible set \([-1,1]^{2n}\) is a hyper‑cube, the maximum of a
quadratic form with a **sign‑symmetric** matrix is attained at a vertex,
i.e. at \(x_i\in\{-1,1\}\).  
(If a coordinate is interior, moving it to an endpoint increases the
value because the derivative has the same sign as the coordinate.)

Hence we may restrict to binary vectors \(\mathbf x\in\{-1,1\}^{2n}\).

### 1.  Reformulation with a “cut”

Define a set  

\[
S=\{i\mid x_i=1\},\qquad \bar S=\{i\mid x_i=-1\}.
\]

Then  

\[
x_r x_s=
\begin{cases}
+1, & r,s\in S\text{ or }r,s\in\bar S,\\
-1, & r\in S,\;s\in\bar S\text{ (or vice‑versa)} .
\end{cases}
\]

Consequently  

\[
F(\mathbf x)=\sum_{1\le r<s\le 2n}c_{s-r}
          \;-\;2\!\!\sum_{\substack{r\in S,\,s\in\bar S\\r<s}}c_{s-r}.
\tag{2}
\]

The first sum is independent of the choice of \(S\); denote it by \(C\).
Thus maximising \(F\) is equivalent to **minimising**  

\[
\Phi(S)=\sum_{\substack{r\in S,\,s\in\bar S\\r<s}}c_{s-r}

\tag{3}
\]

negative and such a pair would *increase* the value of \(F\); we would
never want to count it in \(\Phi\).
