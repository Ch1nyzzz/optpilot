## 1.  What the doors do – the “door‑graph’’  

* The arrangement of the \(n\) lines partitions the plane into  

\[
R_n = \frac{n(n+1)}{2}+1
\]

open regions (the usual formula for the number of cells of a line arrangement).

* Each region is a vertex of a graph \(G\).  
  Two vertices are **adjacent** in the *dual* of the arrangement (the usual adjacency
  across a wall), but knights cannot cross a wall, so this adjacency is irrelevant for
  movement.

* At an intersection of two lines there are four incident regions, forming a 4‑cycle in
  the dual graph.  
  The two opposite regions whose incident sides have *different* colours are joined by a
  two‑way door.  
  Hence **each intersection contributes exactly one edge of \(G\)**, namely one of the two
  diagonals of that 4‑cycle.  
  Which diagonal appears depends on the colours that Merlin chose for the two lines,
  but *exactly one* diagonal is always present.

Thus the “door‑graph’’ \(G\) has  

* vertices = the \(R_n\) regions,
* edges    = the \(\binom{n}{2}\) intersections (one edge per intersection).

Morgana can place at most one knight in each connected component of \(G\); she can always
place exactly one, so the number of knights she can guarantee equals the number of
connected components of \(G\).
