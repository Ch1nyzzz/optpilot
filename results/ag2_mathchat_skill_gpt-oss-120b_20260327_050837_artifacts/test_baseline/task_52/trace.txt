   - First he takes one‑third of one pile: \(\dfrac{1}{3}\times\dfrac{T}{2}= \dfrac{T}{6}\).  
   - Then he gives half of that amount: \(\dfrac{1}{2}\times\dfrac{T}{6}= \dfrac{T}{12}\).

   So the brother receives \(\dfrac{T}{12}\) pins.

3. **Pins left after the gift.**  
   John’s remaining pins are the original total minus what he gave away:
   \[
   T - \frac{T}{12}= \frac{11T}{12}.
   \]

   We are told this remainder equals 66:
   \[
   \frac{11T}{12}=66.
   \]

4. **Solve for \(T\).**  
   Multiply both sides by \(\dfrac{12}{11}\):
   \[
   T = 66 \times \frac{12}{11}= \frac{66}{11}\times12 = 6\times12 = 72.
   \]

5. **Check the result.**  
   - Total pins = 72 → each pile = 36.  
   - One‑third of a pile = 12; half of that = 6.  
   - After giving away 6 pins, John has \(72-6 = 66\) pins left, which matches the problem statement.
