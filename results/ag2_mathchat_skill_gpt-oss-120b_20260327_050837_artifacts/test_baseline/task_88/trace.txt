\[
f\bigl(x-f(y)\bigr)=f\bigl(f(x)\bigr)-f(y)-1\qquad\forall x,y\in\mathbb Z. \tag{1}
\]

A natural first step is to try affine (linear) functions  

\[
f(t)=a t+b,\qquad a,b\in\mathbb Z .
\]

algebraic equations for \(a\) and \(b\). Solving the system yields exactly two
possibilities:

* \(a=0,\;b=-1\) → \(f(t)\equiv -1\);
* \(a=1,\;b=1\) → \(f(t)=t+1\).

Both functions indeed satisfy (1) (a direct substitution confirms it).

A short argument shows that no other (non‑affine) integer‑valued functions can
fulfil (1).

\[
c = f(0).
\]

\[
f\bigl(-f(y)\bigr)=f(c)-f(y)-1. \tag{2}
\]

Replacing \(y\) by \(0\) in (2) yields  

\[
f(-c)=f(c)-c-1. \tag{3}
\]

Now set \(y\) such that \(f(y)=c\) (possible because (2) shows the image of
\(f\) is the whole set of integers shifted by a constant). Using (1) with this

\[

leads to the two solutions already found.
