Number the vertices of the regular 24‑gon \(0,1,\dots ,23\) clockwise.
A segment joining vertex \(i\) to vertex \(j\) has the same length as the
segment joining \(i\) to \(i+k\) where  

\[
k\equiv j-i\pmod{24},\qquad 1\le k\le 12 .
\]

Thus a “segment of a given length’’ is completely described by the step
\(k\) (the smaller of the two possible steps around the circle).  
Our task is therefore:

*Choose a step \(k\) ( \(1\le k\le 12\) ) and count the perfect matchings of the
graph whose edges are all chords of step \(k\).  
Add the numbers for all admissible \(k\).*

--------------------------------------------------------------------

### 1.  The graph for a fixed step \(k\)

For a fixed \(k\) connect each vertex \(i\) with the two vertices
\(i+k\) and \(i-k\) (indices taken modulo \(24\)).
Every vertex has degree 2, so the graph is a disjoint union of cycles.
If  

\[
d=\gcd(24,k),
\]

then the step \(+k\) moves us through a cycle of length  

\[
L=\frac{24}{d}.
\]

Consequently the whole graph consists of \(d\) identical cycles,
each of length \(L\).

--------------------------------------------------------------------

### 2.  When does a perfect matching exist?

A cycle of odd length cannot be covered by disjoint edges, so we need
\(L\) even.  
\(L\) is even  \(\Longleftrightarrow\)  \(\dfrac{24}{d}\) is even
\(\Longleftrightarrow\)  \(d\) is **not** a divisor that contains the factor
\(8\).  
Hence the only forbidden values are  

\[
d=8\quad\text{or}\quad d=24 .
\]

(For \(d=24\) we would have \(k=0\), which does not give a segment.)

--------------------------------------------------------------------
