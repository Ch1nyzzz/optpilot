### 1.  Why a smaller total cannot work  

Assume that Alice starts with fewer than  

\[
S_{60}=1+2+\dots +60 = 1830
\]

pebbles.  
Let  

\[
a_{1},a_{2},\dots ,a_{60}\qquad (a_i\ge 0,\; \sum a_i <S_{60})
\]

be the numbers of pebbles that Alice puts in the boxes at the beginning of the
game.

Because there are only \(59\) different positive integers that are \(\le 60\),
the pigeon‑hole principle tells us that at least two of the boxes must contain
the **same** number of pebbles.  
Choose the leftmost pair of equal boxes; say  

\[
a_{p}=a_{p+1}=m .
\]

Bob now chooses the cut \(k=p\).  
The two groups are  

\[
G_{L}=B_{1},\dots ,B_{p},\qquad   
G_{R}=B_{p+1},\dots ,B_{60}.
\]

Whatever side Alice decides to **increase**, the other side is **decreased**.
Consequently one of the two equal boxes will lose a pebble and become
\(m-1\), while the other will keep the value \(m\).  
Thus after the first round the two boxes have different numbers of pebbles,
and the smaller of them is \(m-1\).

Now look at the next round.  
Bob again chooses the cut that separates the two boxes (the same cut \(k=p\)).
If Alice now decides to increase the side that contains the box with \(m-1\)
pebbles, the other side loses a pebble and the box that previously had \(m\)
becomes \(m-1\) as well.  
If she does the opposite, the box that already has \(m-1\) becomes \(m-2\) and
Bob wins immediately.

Hence, **as soon as two equal boxes appear on opposite sides of a cut,
Bob can force a zero in the next round**.  
With fewer than \(1830\) pebbles such a pair of equal boxes is unavoidable,
so Alice cannot have a winning strategy.

### 1.  Reformulating one round

For a fixed cut `k` define the vector  

```
s(k) = ( 1,…,1 , –1,…,–1 )      (k times 1, 60–k times –1)
```

If Alice decides to *increase* the left part, the configuration changes by `+s(k)`;
if she decides to *increase* the right part, the change is `–s(k)`.

Hence a round is exactly the addition of either `+s(k)` or `–s(k)` to the
current 60‑vector of pebble numbers.

--------------------------------------------------------------------

### 2.  Using differences

```
d_i = a_i – a_{i+1}      ( 1 ≤ i ≤ 59 )
```

be the differences of two consecutive boxes.
From the definition of `s(k)` one checks easily that a round **only**
affects the single difference `d_k` :

```
adding  +s(k)  →  d_k increases by 2
adding  –s(k)  →  d_k decreases by 2
```

All other `d_i` stay unchanged.  
The first box `a_1` itself changes by `±1` in every round (it always belongs
to the left part), and the last box `a_60` changes by `∓1`.
