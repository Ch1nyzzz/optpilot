We have two binary weather events:

- \(R\): “rain”
- \(S\): “sun”

The meteorologist gives us two pieces of information:

1. \(P(R \text{ and } \neg S)=0.30\) (the chance of rain **without** sun)
2. \(P(\neg R)=0.40\) (the chance of **no** rain)

We are asked for the probability of **both** rain **and** sun, i.e. \(P(R \land S)\).

---

### Step‑by‑Step Reasoning

1. **Find the marginal probability of rain**  
   \[
   P(R)=1-P(\neg R)=1-0.40=0.60.
   \]

2. **Express the rain probability in terms of the two mutually exclusive rain scenarios**  
   When it rains, either the sun is present or it isn’t. These two cases are disjoint, so:
   \[
   P(R)=P(R\land S)+P(R\land \neg S).
   \]

3. **Insert the known values**  
   \[
   0.60 = P(R\land S) + 0.30.
   \]

4. **Solve for the desired probability**  
   \[
   P(R\land S)=0.60-0.30 = 0.30.
   \]

5. **Check consistency with the total‑probability rule**  
   The four possible joint outcomes must sum to 1:
   \[
   P(R\land S) + P(R\land \neg S) + P(\neg R\land S) + P(\neg R\land \neg S)=1.
   \]
   We know \(P(R\land \neg S)=0.30\) and \(P(\neg R)=0.40\) (which equals the sum of the last two terms). Hence:
   \[
   P(R\land S) + 0.30 + 0.40 = 1 \;\Longrightarrow\; P(R\land S)=0.30,
   \]
   confirming the result.

---

### Answer

\[
\boxed{0.30}
\]

Thus the correct option is **(C) 0.3**.
