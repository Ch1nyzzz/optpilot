2. **Recall the core idea of each listed normative theory**  

   | Theory | Core principle |
   |--------|----------------|
   | **(A) Hedonistic utilitarianism** | Maximize total *pleasure* (or happiness) for the greatest number of people. It is a single‑criterion, consequentialist approach. |
   | **(B) Preference utilitarianism** | Maximize the satisfaction of *individual preferences* (what people want), again a consequentialist calculus but focused on preference fulfillment. |
   | **(C) The Categorical Imperative** (Kant) | Act only on maxims that can be willed as universal law; emphasizes duty and respect for persons, not outcome maximization. |
   | **(D) Ethics of care** | Emphasizes relational responsibilities, empathy, and caring for particular others; not a calculus of aggregate outcomes. |

3. **Match the structure of profit maximization to the theories**

   - Preference utilitarianism, while also outcome‑oriented, is about satisfying a plurality of *different* preferences, not about a single monetary metric.  
   - The Categorical Imperative and ethics of care are fundamentally *deontological* or *relational* and do not resemble the purely consequentialist, “max‑something” logic of profit maximization.

4. **Select the closest analogue**  
   - The closest analogue among the options is **hedonistic utilitarianism (A)**, because both treat the maximization of a single, quantifiable good (pleasure vs profit) as the guiding principle.
