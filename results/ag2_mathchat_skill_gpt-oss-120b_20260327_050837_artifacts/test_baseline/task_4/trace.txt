### 1.  Geometry of the hexagon  

Let the side length of the equilateral hexagon be \(s\).  
Because opposite sides are parallel and all six sides have the same length, the hexagon is *centrally‑symmetric*: the three vectors that describe three consecutive sides are repeated with opposite sign.

Denote  

\[
\mathbf u = \overrightarrow{AB},\qquad 
\mathbf v = \overrightarrow{BC},\qquad 
\mathbf w = \overrightarrow{CD},
\]

so that  

\[
|\mathbf u|=|\mathbf v|=|\mathbf w| = s .
\]

The remaining sides are  

\[
\overrightarrow{DE}= -\mathbf u,\qquad 
\overrightarrow{EF}= -\mathbf v,\qquad 
\overrightarrow{FA}= -\mathbf w .
\]

Choosing \(A\) as the origin, the vertices are  

\[
\begin{aligned}
A &=\mathbf 0,\\
B &=\mathbf u,\\
C &=\mathbf u+\mathbf v,\\
D &=\mathbf u+\mathbf v+\mathbf w,\\
E &=\mathbf v+\mathbf w,\\
F &=\mathbf w .
\end{aligned}
\]

* \(L_{1}\) – the line through \(A\) and \(B\) (direction \(\mathbf u\));
* \(L_{2}\) – the line through \(C\) and \(D\) (direction \(\mathbf w\));
* \(L_{3}\) – the line through \(E\) and \(F\) (direction \(-\mathbf v\)).

For a convex equilateral hexagon whose opposite sides are parallel, an affine transformation can map it to a regular hexagon while preserving parallelism and ratios of lengths on parallel lines.  
Hence we may work with a regular hexagon of side length \(s\); the triangle formed by the extensions of sides \(AB,\;CD,\;EF\) will have side lengths proportional to \(s\).  
By computing these side lengths for the regular hexagon we obtain a constant factor \(k\) such that  

\[
\text{(triangle side)} = k\,s .
\]

The given triangle has sides \(200,240,300\); all three are multiples of a common factor, so the same factor \(k\) must work for each side.  
From this we solve for \(s\).
