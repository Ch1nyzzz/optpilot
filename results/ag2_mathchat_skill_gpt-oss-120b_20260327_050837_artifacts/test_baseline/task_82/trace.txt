Let \(|S|=m\).  
From a set of \(m\) integers we can obtain at most  

\[
\binom{m}{2}= \frac{m(m-1)}{2}
\]

different positive differences (the unordered pairs \(\{x,y\}\) with \(x>y\)).  
For the problem we need the \(n-1\) distinct numbers  

\[
F_{2},F_{3},\dots ,F_{n}.
\]

Hence we must have  

\[
\frac{m(m-1)}{2}\ge n-1\qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad 
m\ge \frac{1+\sqrt{1+8(n-1)}}{2}.
\tag{1}
\]

Formula (1) gives a *numerical* lower bound, but it is not sufficient: the
required differences are not arbitrary – they are the Fibonacci numbers.
A stronger restriction comes from the fact that every Fibonacci number is
the sum of two *consecutive* earlier Fibonacci numbers:

\[
F_{k}=F_{k-1}+F_{k-2}\qquad(k\ge 3).
\tag{2}
\]

If we write the elements of \(S\) in increasing order  

\[
s_{1}<s_{2}<\dots <s_{m},
\]

the difference \(s_{i}-s_{j}\;(i>j)\) is the sum of the **consecutive**
gaps  

\[
(s_{j+1}-s_{j})+(s_{j+2}-s_{j+1})+\dots +(s_{i}-s_{i-1}).
\tag{3}
\]

Thus every required Fibonacci number must be representable as a sum of a
*consecutive* block of the gaps  

\[
d_{1}=s_{2}-s_{1},\;d_{2}=s_{3}-s_{2},\dots ,d_{m-1}=s_{m}-s_{m-1}.
\tag{4}
\]

Consequently we need a sequence \((d_{1},d_{2},\dots ,d_{m-1})\) such that
each of the \(n-1\) numbers \(F_{2},\dots ,F_{n}\) appears as a sum of a
consecutive sub‑sequence of the \(d\)’s.
