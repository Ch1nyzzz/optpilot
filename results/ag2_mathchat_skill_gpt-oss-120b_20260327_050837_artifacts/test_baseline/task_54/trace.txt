1. **Legal standard for admissibility of a confession**  
   - A confession is admissible only if it is *voluntary* under the Due Process Clause (and, when Miranda‑required, also “knowing and intelligent”).  
   - If a confession is *involuntary*—e.g., obtained through coercion, threats, or improper inducement—it is **inadmissible**.

2. **Assess each answer choice**  
   - **(A) The defendant's statement was involuntary.**  
     – Directly violates the voluntariness requirement → inadmissible.  
   - **(B) The defendant's statement was voluntary.**  
     – Satisfies the core requirement → admissible (assuming no other defect).  
   - **(C) The defendant was not in custody when the statement was made.**  
     – Custody is relevant for Miranda warnings, but a non‑custodial, voluntary statement can still be admitted.  
   - **(D) The statement was not made in response to a known police interrogation.**  
     – If the statement is voluntary and not the product of interrogation, it remains admissible; lack of interrogation does not per se bar admission.

3. **Conclusion**  
   The only scenario that automatically renders a confession inadmissible is when the confession is **involuntary**.

**Answer:** **(A) The defendant's statement was involuntary.**
