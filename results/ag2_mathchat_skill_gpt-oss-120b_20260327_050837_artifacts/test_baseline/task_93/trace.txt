### 1.  Why a 1‑good function cannot exist  

Assume that a function \(f:\mathbb Z_{>0}\to\mathbb Z_{>0}\) satisfies  

\[
\gcd\bigl(f(m)+n,\;f(n)+m\bigr)=1\qquad\text{for all }m\neq n .
\tag{1}
\]

Take any prime \(p\).  
Consider the residues of the numbers \(f(1),f(2),\dots ,f(p)\) modulo \(p\).  
There are only \(p\) possible residues, so by the pigeon‑hole principle two
different indices \(a,b\in\{1,\dots ,p\}\) must satisfy  

\[
f(a)\equiv -b\pmod p ,\qquad   f(b)\equiv -a\pmod p .
\tag{2}
\]

Indeed, if for some \(a\) we set \(r_a\equiv f(a)+a\pmod p\), then (2) is
equivalent to \(r_a\equiv r_b\pmod p\); two equal residues give a pair
\((a,b)\) with the required property.

From (2) we obtain  

\[
p\mid f(a)+b ,\qquad p\mid f(b)+a ,
\]

hence \(p\mid\gcd\bigl(f(a)+b,\;f(b)+a\bigr)\).
Because we can choose the prime \(p\) arbitrarily large, the
greatest common divisor of the two numbers can be made arbitrarily large.
Consequently (1) cannot hold for all pairs \((m,n)\); a 1‑good function does
not exist.

### 1.  A necessary condition on \(k\)

Fix a positive integer \(t\).  
Consider the residues of the ordered pair  

\[
\bigl(m\bmod t,\;f(m)\bmod t\bigr)
\]

There are only \(t^{2}\) possible residue pairs, therefore by the
pigeon‑hole principle there exist distinct integers \(a\neq b\) such that  

\[
a\equiv b\pmod t ,\qquad f(a)\equiv f(b)\pmod t . \tag{1}
\]

Write \(a=b+qt\) with \(q\ge 1\).  
From (1) we have  

\[
f(a)=f(b)+rt\qquad\text{for some }r\in\mathbb Z .
\]

Now compute the two numbers whose gcd we are interested in:

\[
\begin{aligned}
f(a)+b &= f(b)+rt+b ,\\[2mm]
f(b)+a &= f(b)+b+qt .
\end{aligned}
\]

Both are congruent to \(f(b)+b\pmod t\); consequently  

\[
\gcd\bigl(f(a)+b,\;f(b)+a\bigr)\ge \gcd\bigl(t,\;f(b)+b\bigr). \tag{2}
\]

\(f(b)+b\), then the right–hand side of (2) equals \(1\); otherwise it
equals that prime.  
Thus, for every prime \(p\) we can find a pair \((a,b)\) with  

\[
\gcd\bigl(f(a)+b,\;f(b)+a\bigr)\ge p .
\]

Hence the set \(\{g(m,n):m\neq n\}\) is **unbounded** for *any* function
\(f\).  
Consequently no finite integer \(k\) can dominate all these gcd’s.
