### 1.  What a “company’’ really does  

For one company let  

\[
(s_{1},t_{1}),\;(s_{2},t_{2}),\dots ,(s_{k},t_{k})
\qquad (s_{i}<t_{i})
\]

be the \(k\) cable‑car rides.  
The hypothesis  

\[
s_{1}<s_{2}<\dots <s_{k},\qquad   
t_{1}<t_{2}<\dots <t_{k}
\]

means that the rides are ordered from the bottom of the mountain to the top:
the \(i\)-th ride starts lower than the \((i+1)\)-st one and also finishes lower.

A traveller may use several rides in succession, but he can change from one
ride to the next **only when the end of the first ride coincides with the start
of the next one**.  
Consequently the rides of a company decompose into *chains* :

```
s_i = c_0  →  t_i = c_1  = s_{i+1} →  t_{i+1}=c_2 = s_{i+2} → … →  t_{j}=c_m .
```

Inside a chain every lower checkpoint can reach every higher checkpoint.
If a ride does **not** meet the start of the next ride, it forms a chain of
length 1 (an isolated ride).

Thus for a company the set of “linked’’ pairs of checkpoints is exactly the
set of all ordered pairs \((c_{p},c_{q})\) that belong to the same chain and
\(p<q\).
