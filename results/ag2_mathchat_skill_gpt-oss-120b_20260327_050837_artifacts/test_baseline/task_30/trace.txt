(B) Even if the probabilities are truncated at zero and one, there will probably be many observations for which the probability is either exactly zero or exactly one
(C) The error terms will be heteroscedastic and not normally distributed
(D) The model is much harder to estimate than a standard regressi
Output: **Step‑by‑step reasoning**

1. **Recall what the Linear Probability Model (LPM) is**  
   - It is an ordinary least‑squares (OLS) regression where the dependent variable \(Y\) is binary (0/1) and the model is written as  
     \[
     Y_i = X_i\beta + u_i .
     \]  
   - Because OLS is used, the usual OLS assumptions (linearity, exogeneity, etc.) are imposed, but the special nature of a binary outcome creates a few well‑known quirks.

2. **Examine each statement**

   **(A) “There is nothing in the model to ensure that the estimated probabilities lie between zero and one.”**  
   - In OLS the fitted value is simply \( \hat Y_i = X_i\hat\beta\). Nothing restricts this linear combination to the \([0,1]\) interval.  
   - **This statement is true.**

   **(B) “Even if the probabilities are truncated at zero and one, there will probably be many observations for which the probability is either exactly zero or exactly one.”**  
   - If we force any predicted value below 0 to 0 and any above 1 to 1 (a common ad‑hoc fix), the resulting “truncated” predictions can indeed pile up at the boundaries, especially when the linear predictor frequently falls outside the interval. Empirical work often finds a non‑trivial number of such boundary observations.  
   - **This statement is generally true (or at least plausible).**

   **(C) “The error terms will be heteroscedastic and not normally distributed.”**  
   - For a binary outcome, the error term is \(u_i = Y_i - X_i\beta\). Its variance is  
     \[
     \operatorname{Var}(u_i|X_i)=p_i(1-p_i),
     \]  
     where \(p_i = P(Y_i=1|X_i)\). Because \(p_i\) varies with \(X_i\), the variance is not constant → heteroscedasticity. Moreover, with only two possible values (0 or 1), the distribution of \(u_i\) is far from normal.  
   - **This statement is true.**

   **(D) “The model is much harder to estimate than a standard regression model with a continuous dependent variable.”**  
   - The LPM is estimated by ordinary least squares
