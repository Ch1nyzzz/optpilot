Output: **Solution (independent of any other agents)**  

1. **Identify the sample space**  
   - For each meal (breakfast, lunch, dinner) Joe chooses independently among three fruits: apple (A), orange (O), banana (B).  
   - Number of possible fruit‑sequences for the day = \(3 \times 3 \times 3 = 3^{3}=27\).  
   - All 27 sequences are equally likely because each choice is made with equal probability \(1/3\).

2. **Define the event of interest**  
   - We want the probability that **at least two different kinds of fruit** are eaten during the day.  
   - The complement of this event is the situation where **all three meals are the same fruit** (i.e., only one kind of fruit is eaten all day).

3. **Count the complement outcomes**  
   - All‑same‑fruit sequences:  
     * A‑A‑A  
     * O‑O‑O  
     * B‑B‑B  
   - There are exactly \(3\) such sequences.

4. **Compute the probability**  
   \[
   P(\text{at least two different fruits}) = 1 - P(\text{all three the same}) 
   = 1 - \frac{3}{27}
   = 1 - \frac{1}{9}
   = \frac{8}{9}.
   \]

5. **Select the answer choice**  
   The fraction \(\frac{8}{9}\) corresponds to option **(B)**.

---

**Answer:** \(\boxed{\frac{8}{9}}\) (Option B)  

---

*Discussion:*  
The problem reduces to a simple complement count because the only way Joe fails to eat “at least two different kinds of fruit” is to eat the same fruit for all three meals. With three fruit choices, there are three such uniform sequences out of the total 27 possible sequences, giving the result \(1 - 3/27 = 8/9\). This reasoning is straightforward and does not require enumerating all 24 favorable cases individually.
