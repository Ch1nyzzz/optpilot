Task: We are given an infinite deck of cards, each with a real number on it. For every real number $x$, there is exactly one card in the deck that has $x$ written on it. Now two players draw disjoint sets $A$ and $B$ of 100 cards each from this deck. We would like to define a rule that declares one of them a winner. This rule should satisfy the following conditions:

1. The winner only depends on the relative order of the 200 cards: if the cards are laid down in increasing order face down and we are told which card belongs to which player, but not what numbers are written on them, we can still decide the winner.
2. If we write the elements of both sets in increasing order as $A=\left\{a_{1}, a_{2}, \ldots, a_{100}\right\}$ and $B=\left\{b_{1}, b_{2}, \ldots, b_{100}\right\}$, and $a_{i}>b_{i}$ for all $i$, then $A$ beats $B$.
3. If three players draw three disjoint sets $A, B, C$ from the deck, $A$ beats $B$ and $B$ beats $C$, then $A$ also beats $C$.

How many ways are there to define such a rule? Here, we consider two rules as different if there exist two sets $A$ and $B$ such that $A$ beats $B$ according to one rule, but $B$ beats $A$ according to the other.

### 1.  What a “rule’’ really is  

For a pair of disjoint 100‑card sets \(A,B\) write the 200 cards in increasing order and replace each card by the letter of the player who owns it.  
Thus a situation is represented by a binary word  

\[
w=w_{1}w_{2}\dots w_{200},\qquad w_i\in\{A,B\},
\]

containing exactly 100 letters \(A\) and 100 letters \(B\).  
A rule is a function  

\[
f:\{\,\text{binary words of length }200\text{ with }100\;A\text{'s}\,\}\longrightarrow\{A\text{ wins},B\text{ wins}\}
\]

that never declares a tie.

--------------------------------------------------------------------

### 2.  The dominance condition  

Write the elements of the two sets in increasing order  

\[
A=\{a_1<a_2<\dots<a_{100}\},\qquad 
B=\{b_1<b_2<\dots<b_{100}\}.
\]

The condition  

\[
a_i>b_i\quad\text{for all }i
\]

means that when we scan the ordered 200 cards from the smallest upward,
at every moment we have seen **at least as many** \(B\)’s as \(A\)’s.
In the word \(w\) this is exactly the *ballot* (or Dyck) condition  

\[
\#A\text{ in the first }k\text{ letters}\le \#B\text{ in the first }k\text{ letters}
\qquad(k=1,\dots ,200).
\]

Hence condition 2 forces

\[
\text{if }w\text{ never has more }A\text{ than }B\text{ in any prefix, then }A\text{ wins}.
\tag{1}
\]

By swapping the roles of the players we also obtain the symmetric statement:

\[
\text{if }w\text{ never has more }B\text{ than }A\text{ in any prefix, then }B\text{ wins}.
\tag{2}
\]

--------------------------------------------------------------------
