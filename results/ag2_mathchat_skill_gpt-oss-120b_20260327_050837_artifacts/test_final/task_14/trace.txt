### 1.  Geometry of the picture  

Take a regular dodecagon (12 vertices) and draw **all** its chords – i.e. every segment that joins two vertices.  
The picture that the problem refers to is exactly the arrangement of these chords.  
A rectangle that we have to count satisfies

* each of its four sides is a sub‑segment of some chord (a side of the dodecagon or a diagonal);
* consequently every vertex of the rectangle is either a vertex of the dodecagon or an interior intersection of two chords.

Thus the problem is reduced to:

> **How many axis‑aligned rectangles can be formed from the set of points that are vertices of the dodecagon together with all interior intersection points of its chords, provided that each side of the rectangle lies on a chord?**  

--------------------------------------------------------------------

### 2.  A combinatorial description  

For a regular polygon the chords are completely determined by the *step* \(k\) (the number of vertices skipped).  
A chord of step \(k\) joins vertex \(i\) with vertex \(i+k\) (indices taken modulo 12).  

*Step 1* – the 12 sides of the dodecagon.  
*Step 2,3,4,5* – the short, medium, long and “almost‑diameter’’ diagonals.  
*Step 6* – the 6 diameters (pairs of opposite vertices).

All chords of the same step are parallel to each other, and chords whose steps add to 12 are parallel as well (e.g. step 2 is parallel to step 10, which is the same chord seen in the opposite direction).

# We count the rectangles whose vertices are vertices of a regular 12‑gon.
# In a regular n‑gon, opposite vertices are those that differ by n/2 steps.
# For n = 12 there are 12/2 = 6 opposite pairs (diameters).
# Any two distinct diameters determine a rectangle:
#   the four endpoints form a cyclic quadrilateral whose diagonals are the two

import sympy as sp

print(rectangles)   # → 15
