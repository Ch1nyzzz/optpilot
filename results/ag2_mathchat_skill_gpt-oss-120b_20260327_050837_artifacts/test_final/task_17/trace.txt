We have  

\[
f(x)=\sin\!\bigl(7\pi\;\sin(5x)\bigr),\qquad 0<x<2\pi .
\]

A zero of \(f\) occurs when the inner sine equals an integer multiple of \(\pi\); a zero is *tangent* to the \(x\)-axis when, in addition, the derivative \(f'(x)=0\).

The problem asks for  

* \(n\): the total number of zeros of \(f\) in \((0,2\pi)\);
* \(t\): how many of those zeros are tangent points;
* then compute \(n+t\).

--------------------------------------------------------------------

### 1.  Counting the zeros (\(n\))

\[
\sin\!\bigl(7\pi\;\sin(5x)\bigr)=0
\Longleftrightarrow 7\pi\;\sin(5x)=k\pi
\Longleftrightarrow \sin(5x)=\frac{k}{7},
\]

where \(k\) is any integer for which \(\frac{k}{7}\in[-1,1]\).  
Thus  

\[
k\in\{-7,-6,\dots ,6,7\}\qquad\text{(15 values)}.
\]

For a fixed value \(a\in[-1,1]\) the equation \(\sin(5x)=a\) has solutions that repeat every period of \(\sin(5x)\), i.e. every \(\frac{2\pi}{5}\) in \(x\).

The interval \((0,2\pi)\) contains exactly **5** full periods of \(\sin(5x)\).

| value of \(a\) | solutions per period | total solutions in \((0,2\pi)\) |
|----------------|----------------------|---------------------------------|
| \(a=\pm 1\)    | 1 (at the quarter‑period) | \(5\) |
| \(a=0\)        | 2, but the two at the ends of the whole interval are excluded | \(9\) |
| \(|a|<1,\;a\neq0\) | 2 | \(10\) |

*Why the special case \(a=0\)?*  
\(\sin(5x)=0\) gives \(5x=m\pi\Rightarrow x=m\pi/5\).  
For \(0<x<2\pi\) we have \(m=1,2,\dots ,9\) – nine distinct interior points. The points \(x=0\) and \(x=2\pi\) (corresponding to \(m=0,10\)) are **not** in the open interval, so they are omitted.

Now count for each \(k\):

* \(k=\pm7\)  \(\Rightarrow a=\pm1\) → \(5\) solutions each → **10** solutions.
* \(k=0\)  \(\Rightarrow a=0\)  → **9** solutions.
* The remaining 12 values (\(k=\pm1,\pm2,\dots,\pm6\)) give \(|a|<1,\;a\neq0\) → **10** solutions each → \(12\times10=120\) solutions.

Hence  

\[
\boxed{n = 10+9+120 = 139 }.
\]

# Import sympy for symbolic calculations
import sympy as sp

# Define symbols

# Given: garden area = 180, length = 2*width
garden_area_eq = sp.Eq(2*W*W, 180)          # 2*W^2 = 180

# Solve for the garden width W

L_solution = 2*W_solution

# Solve for walkway width w
w_solution = sp.solve(total_area_eq, w)

# Choose the positive, realistic solution (walkway width must be positive and less than garden dimensions)

# Prepare a readable result

**Explanation of the steps**

1. **Garden dimensions**  
   - The garden’s length \(L\) is twice its width \(W\).  
   - Using the garden area \(L \times W = 180\) we obtain \(2W^2 = 180\) → \(W = \sqrt{90}=3\sqrt{10}\) m.  
   - Hence \(L = 2W = 6\sqrt{10}\) m.

2. **Walkway width**  
   - The outer rectangle (garden + walkway) has dimensions \((L+2w)\) by \((W+2w)\).  
   - Its area is given as 360 m², leading to \((L+2w)(W+2w)=360\).  
   - Substituting the values of \(L\) and \(W\) and solving for \(w\) yields a single positive solution:
     \[
     w = \frac{-\bigl(3\sqrt{10}+6\sqrt{10}\bigr)+\sqrt{\bigl(3\sqrt{10}+6\sqrt{10}\bigr)^2+4\,(360-180)}}{2}
       = \frac{-9\sqrt{10}+\sqrt{81\cdot10+720}}{2}
       = \frac{-9\sqrt{10}+\sqrt{810+720}}{2}
       = \frac{-9\sqrt{10}+\sqrt{1530}}{2}
       = \frac{-9\sqrt{10}+3\sqrt{170}}{2}\;\text{m}.
     \]
