For a point \(c=(x,y)\) its *neighborhood* \(N(c)\) is the set of lattice points

Hence  

\[
|N(c)|=(2n+1)^2-1 = 4n(n+1),\qquad
\frac{|N(c)|}{2}=2n(n+1).
\]

A butterfly is  

* **lonely**   – fewer than \(2n(n+1)\) butterflies in its neighbourhood,  
* **crowded** – more than \(2n(n+1)\) butterflies in its neighbourhood,  
* **comfortable** – exactly \(2n(n+1)\) butterflies in its neighbourhood.

Every minute all lonely butterflies disappear simultaneously.
The process stops when no lonely butterfly remains.
We are asked for the number of comfortable butterflies in the final configuration.

### 1.  Geometry of the neighbourhood

For a point \(c=(x,y)\) the square \(S(c)\) of side \(2n+1\) consists of the points  

\[
\{(i,j)\mid |i-x|\le n,\;|j-y|\le n\}.
\]

Only points with non‑negative coordinates can ever contain a butterfly,
so the *effective* neighbourhood of \(c\) is  

\[
E(c)=S(c)\cap\{(i,j)\mid i\ge 0,\;j\ge 0\}\setminus\{c\}.
\]

If the whole square lies inside the first quadrant, then
\(|E(c)|=|N(c)|\) and the point is **crowded** (it sees all butterflies).
If the square sticks out of the quadrant, some of its cells are empty,
and the number of butterflies seen by \(c\) is exactly the number of
lattice points of the square that are still present.

Consequently a point can become lonely only when its square is
*partially* outside the first quadrant, i.e. when it is close to one of the
axes.
