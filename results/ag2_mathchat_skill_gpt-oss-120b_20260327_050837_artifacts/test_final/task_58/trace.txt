   - It is a *consequentialist* approach: actions are judged by the outcome (higher profit).

2. **Examine the listed normative theories**  
   - **(A) Hedonistic utilitarianism** – maximizes *pleasure* or *happiness* for the greatest number.  
   - **(B) Preference utilitarianism** – maximizes the *satisfaction of preferences* (or desires) of individuals.  
   - **(C) The Categorical Imperative** – a deontological (duty‑based) theory; actions are right if they can be universalized, irrespective of outcomes.  
   - **(D) Ethics of care** – emphasizes relational responsibilities and caring, not outcome‑oriented metrics.

3. **Match the nature of profit maximization to the closest theory**  
   - Profit maximization is **outcome‑oriented** (consequentialist) but does **not** aim at happiness per se, so it does not align with *hedonistic* utilitarianism.  
   - It also does **not** prescribe duties or caring relationships, ruling out the Categorical Imperative and ethics of care.  
   - The closest match is **preference utilitarianism**, which judges actions by how well they satisfy the relevant preferences. In a corporate context, the primary preference being satisfied is that of shareholders (and often other stakeholders) for higher financial returns. Thus, profit maximization can be seen as a narrow, preference‑based consequentialist rule.

4. **Select the answer**  
   - The normative theory most similar to corporate profit maximization among the options is **(B) preference utilitarianism**.
