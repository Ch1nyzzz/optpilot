For a set \(A\) of positive integers write  

\[
\operatorname{lcm}(A_{1}) = \operatorname{gcd}(A_{2})
\qquad (A_{1}\cup A_{2}=A,\;A_{1},A_{2}\neq\varnothing )
\tag{1}
\]

A partition satisfying (1) is called **good**.  
We have to find the smallest possible size \(n\) of a set that admits exactly
\(2015\) good partitions.

--------------------------------------------------------------------

### 1.  Reducing the problem to exponents of a single prime  

Take a prime \(p\) and let every element of \(A\) be a power of \(p\):

\[
A=\{p^{e_{1}},p^{e_{2}},\dots ,p^{e_{n}}\},\qquad e_{i}\ge 1 .
\]

For a subset \(B\subseteq A\)

\[
\operatorname{lcm}(B)=p^{\max\{e_i\;:\;p^{e_i}\in B\}},\qquad
\operatorname{gcd}(B)=p^{\min\{e_i\;:\;p^{e_i}\in B\}} .
\]

Hence a partition \((A_{1},A_{2})\) is good iff  

\[
\max\{e_i\;:\;p^{e_i}\in A_{1}\}= \min\{e_i\;:\;p^{e_i}\in A_{2}\}=t
\tag{2}
\]

for some exponent \(t\).  
Consequences of (2):

* every element whose exponent is **smaller** than \(t\) must lie in \(A_{1}\);
* every element whose exponent is **larger** than \(t\) must lie in \(A_{2}\);
* the elements whose exponent equals \(t\) are split between the two parts,
  and each side must receive at least one of them.

If the exponent \(t\) occurs \(m_t\) times in the multiset \(\{e_i\}\), then the
number of ways to split those \(m_t\) equal elements into two non‑empty groups
is  

\[
2^{\,m_t}-2 .
\]

Thus each distinct exponent value contributes exactly \(2^{m_t}-2\) good
partitions, and different exponent values give disjoint families of good
partitions.  

\[
\boxed{\displaystyle
\text{#good partitions}= \sum_{t\in T}\bigl(2^{m_t}-2\bigr)}
\tag{3}
\]

where \(T\) is the set of distinct exponents appearing in \(A\).
