Output: **Solution**

1. Let the original number of pins be \(T\).

2. John splits the pins into two equal piles, so each pile contains  
   \[
   \frac{T}{2}\text{ pins}.
   \]

3. He gives his brother *one‑half of one‑third* of **one** pile.  
   - One‑third of a pile: \(\displaystyle \frac{1}{3}\times\frac{T}{2}= \frac{T}{6}\).  
   - One‑half of that amount: \(\displaystyle \frac{1}{2}\times\frac{T}{6}= \frac{T}{12}\).

   So the number of pins given away is \(\displaystyle \frac{T}{12}\).

4. After giving away those pins John has 66 pins left:
   \[
   T - \frac{T}{12}=66.
   \]

5. Solve for \(T\):
   \[
   \frac{11}{12}T = 66 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
   T = 66 \times \frac{12}{11}=66 \times \frac{12}{11}=72.
   \]

6. Verify:  
   - Total pins = 72 → each pile = 36.  
   - One‑third of a pile = 12; half of that = 6.  
   - 72 − 6 = 66, which matches the condition.

**Answer:** **(B) 72**.
