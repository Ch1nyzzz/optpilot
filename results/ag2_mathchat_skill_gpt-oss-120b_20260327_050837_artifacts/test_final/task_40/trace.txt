1. **Interpret each statement**  

   - **(A)** *“The population is the total collection of all items of interest.”*  
     This is the standard definition of a statistical population. → **True**.  

   - **(B)** *“The population can be infinite.”*  
     In many theoretical contexts (e.g., all possible measurements, all future customers) the population is modeled as infinite. → **True**.  

   - **(C)** *“In theory, the sample could be larger than the population.”*  
     A *sample* is a subset drawn from the population. By definition it cannot contain more *distinct* elements than exist in the population. Even when sampling **with replacement**, the *size* of the sample may exceed the population size, but the sample still consists of repeated draws of the same population elements; it is not a larger *set* of distinct items. In classical survey‑sampling theory the statement “sample could be larger than the population” is considered **false**.  

   - **(D)** *“A random sample is one where each individual item from the population is equally likely to be drawn.”*  
     This is the textbook definition of a simple random sample. → **True**.  

2. **Identify the false statement**  

   Only statement **(C)** conflicts with the usual definition of a sample being a subset of the population. Therefore (C) is the false statement.

---

**Answer**

The false statement is **(C) In theory, the sample could be larger than the population**.
