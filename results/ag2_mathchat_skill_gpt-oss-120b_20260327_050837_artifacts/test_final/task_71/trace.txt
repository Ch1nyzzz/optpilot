### 1.  What the problem really asks  

We start with  

\[
\prod_{i=1}^{2016}(x-i)=\prod_{i=1}^{2016}(x-i) .
\]

We may delete some factors on the left‑hand side and (possibly different) factors on the right‑hand side, but after the deletions each side must still contain at least one factor.  
If after the deletions the equation  

\[
\prod_{i\in A}(x-i)=\prod_{j\in B}(x-j) \qquad (A,B\subset\{1,\dots ,2016\},
\;|A|,|B|\ge 1)
\]

has **no real solution**, we call the deletion successful.  
The question is: *what is the smallest possible total number of factors that have to be erased?*  

--------------------------------------------------------------------

### 2.  Notation  

* \(A\) – the set of indices that remain on the left,
* \(B\) – the set of indices that remain on the right,
* \(k=|A|=|B|\) – the number of factors kept on each side (we shall see that the two numbers must be equal).

The total number of erased factors is  

\[
E= (2016-|A|)+(2016-|B|)=4032-2k .
\]

Thus **the larger \(k\) is, the fewer factors we erase**.  
We have to find the largest possible \(k\) for which a deletion with *no real root* exists.

--------------------------------------------------------------------

### 3.  Necessary conditions on \(k\)

Let  

\[
f(x)=\prod_{i\in A}(x-i),\qquad 
g(x)=\prod_{j\in B}(x-j).
\]

Both are monic polynomials.  

*If \(|A|\neq|B|\)*, then \(\deg f\neq\deg g\).  
For large positive \(x\) both are positive, while for large negative \(x\) they have opposite signs (because one of the degrees is odd and the other even).  
Hence by the Intermediate Value Theorem the equation \(f(x)=g(x)\) would have a real root.  
Therefore we **must have \(|A|=|B|=k\)**.

\]
