Recall a basic fact about cyclic subgroups:

- If a group \(G\) contains an element \(x\) of order \(n\), then \(\langle x\rangle\) is a cyclic subgroup of order \(n\).
- A cyclic group of order \(n\) has exactly \(\varphi(n)\) generators, where \(\varphi\) is Euler’s totient function.  
  Those generators are precisely the elements of order \(n\) inside that subgroup.

Thus the number of elements of order \(n\) in any group is a multiple of \(\varphi(n)\).

### Step‑by‑step reasoning for order 15

1. **Compute \(\varphi(15)\).**  
   \[
   15 = 3 \times 5,\qquad 
   \varphi(15)=\varphi(3)\varphi(5) = (3-1)(5-1)=2\cdot4 = 8.
   \]

2. **Interpret the meaning of \(\varphi(15)=8\).**  
   Every cyclic subgroup of order 15 contributes **exactly 8** elements of order 15 (its generators).

3. **Statement 1:** “If a group has an element of order 15 it must have at least 8 elements of order 15.”  
   - Having one element of order 15 guarantees the existence of a cyclic subgroup of order 15.  
   - That subgroup supplies its 8 generators, all of order 15.  
   - Hence the group contains **at least** those 8 elements.  
   - **Conclusion:** Statement 1 is **True**.

4. **Statement 2:** “If a group has more than 8 elements of order 15, it must have at least 16 elements of order 15.”  
   - The total number of elements of order 15 must be a multiple of 8 (from step 2).  
   - The possible multiples are \(0,8,16,24,\dots\).  
   - “More than 8” excludes the case 8, so the next admissible multiple is 16.  
   - Therefore any group with >8 such elements actually has ≥16.
