**Reasoning**

| Statement | True / False | Why |
|-----------|--------------|-----|
| (A) Asymptotically, the values of the two test statistics will be equal | **True** | For large \(n\), \((n‑k)\approx n\) so Ljung‑Box’s \(Q^{*}=n(n+2)\sum r_k^{2}/(n‑k)\) reduces to Box‑Pierce’s \(Q=n\sum r_k^{2}\). |
| (B) The Q test has better small‑sample properties than the Q* | **False** | The Ljung‑Box statistic was introduced precisely to improve the small‑sample approximation to the \(\chi^{2}\) distribution; the original Box‑Pierce test is known to be oversized in small samples. |
| (C) The Q test is sometimes over‑sized for small samples | **True** | Empirically, the Box‑Pierce test rejects too often (size > nominal) when \(n\) is modest. |
| (D) As the sample size tends towards infinity, both tests will show a tendency to always reject the null hypothesis of zero autocorrelation coefficients. | **True (in the sense of consistency)** | As \(n\to\infty\) the tests become consistent: under any fixed alternative (i.e., when the true autocorrelations are non‑zero) the power approaches 1, so they will eventually reject the null. |
