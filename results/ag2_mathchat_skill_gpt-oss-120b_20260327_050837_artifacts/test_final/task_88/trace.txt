--------------------------------------------------------------------

### 1.  Preliminary notation  

Write  

\[
P(x,y):\;f\bigl(x-f(y)\bigr)=f\bigl(f(x)\bigr)-f(y)-1 .
\]

Put  

\[
c:=f(0),\qquad K:=f(c)-1 .
\]

--------------------------------------------------------------------

### 2.  A first consequence  

Take \(x=0\) in \(P(x,y)\):

\[
f\bigl(-f(y)\bigr)=f\bigl(f(0)\bigr)-f(y)-1=K-f(y) .
\tag{2}
\]

Thus for every value \(t\) belonging to the image of \(f\),

\[
\boxed{f(-t)=K-t}\qquad(t\in\operatorname{Im}f). \tag{3}
\]

--------------------------------------------------------------------

### 3.  Either \(f\) is constant or it is surjective  

Assume that \(f\) is **not** constant.  
Then there exist \(u,v\in\mathbb Z\) with \(f(u)\neq f(v)\).

From (3) we have \(K-f(u)=f(-f(u))\) and \(K-f(v)=f(-f(v))\); hence the
right‑hand sides are different, so the left‑hand sides are different.
Consequently the set \(\operatorname{Im}f\) contains at least two distinct
integers, and by (3) it also contains the numbers \(K-t\) for every
\(t\in\operatorname{Im}f\).  
Thus \(\operatorname{Im}f\) is closed under the map \(t\mapsto K-t\) and
contains infinitely many elements; in particular it is **unbounded**.

Now fix any integer \(m\).  
Because \(\operatorname{Im}f\) is unbounded, we can choose \(y\) with
\(f(y)>m\).  
From (2),

\[
f\bigl(-f(y)\bigr)=K-f(y)<K-m .
\]

Since the left–hand side is a value of \(f\), the image of \(f\) contains
numbers arbitrarily small.  
Hence the image is both unbounded above and below; therefore it must be
the whole set \(\mathbb Z\).  

Thus **if \(f\) is not constant, it is surjective**.
