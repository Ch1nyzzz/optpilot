For a labyrinth that consists of \(n\) non‑parallel, pairwise non‑concurrent straight lines the quantity  

\[
k(\mathfrak L)=\min_{\text{colourings}}\bigl\{\text{number of connected components of the “door‑graph’’}\bigr\}
\]

does **not depend on the geometric position of the lines** – it depends only on the
number \(n\) of lines.  
For every \(n\ge 1\)

\[
\boxed{\;k(\mathfrak L)=n+1\;}
\]

and consequently the only possible value of \(k(\mathfrak L)\) for a given
\(n\) is \(n+1\).

### 1.  What the knights can do  

When the \(i\)-th wall is painted, we label the side that is painted **red**
by the sign “\(+\)’’ and the side that is painted **blue** by the sign “\(-\)’’.  
Every point of the plane (hence every region of the arrangement) is then
described by a sign‑vector  

\[
\mathbf s=(s_{1},s_{2},\dots ,s_{n})\qquad s_{i}\in\{+,-\},
\]

where \(s_{i}=+\) means that the point lies on the red side of wall \(i\).

Two regions meet at the intersection of walls \(i\) and \(j\).  
At that intersection there are four corners, corresponding to the four
possibilities \((s_{i},s_{j})\in\{(+,+),(+,-),(-,+),(-,-)\}\).

*The door* is placed between the two **diagonally opposite** corners whose
two incident half‑planes have *different* colours.  
In the language of signs this means that the door connects the two regions
whose sign‑vectors differ **exactly in the two coordinates \(i\) and \(j\) and
the two differing signs are opposite**:

\[
(s_{1},\dots ,s_{i},\dots ,s_{j},\dots ,s_{n})
\;\longleftrightarrow\;
(s_{1},\dots ,\overline{s_{i}},\dots ,\overline{s_{j}},\dots ,s_{n}),
\tag{1}
\]

where \(\overline{+}=-\) and \(\overline{-}=+\).

Thus the “door‑graph’’ \(G\) has as vertices the regions of the arrangement,
and an edge is present exactly when the two corresponding sign‑vectors are
related by (1) for some pair \(\{i,j\}\).
