### 1.  Why a smaller \(n\) is impossible  

* **\(n=k+1\).**  
  If the set has exactly \(k+1\) elements, then for every element the only
  possible choice of \(k\) distinct “other’’ elements is the whole complement.

  \[
  a_i=\sum_{j\neq i}a_j .
  \]

  Adding the equations gives \(2\sum a_i = (k+1)\sum a_i\), i.e.  
  \((k-1)\sum a_i =0\).  Since \(k\ge 2\) we obtain \(\sum a_i =0\) and then
  \(a_i=0\) for every \(i\); the numbers are not distinct.  Thus no set of
  size \(k+1\) works.

* **\(n=k+2\).**

  \[

  \]

  x_{\sigma(i)}=S-2x_i ,\qquad S:=\sum_{j=1}^{k+2}x_j .
