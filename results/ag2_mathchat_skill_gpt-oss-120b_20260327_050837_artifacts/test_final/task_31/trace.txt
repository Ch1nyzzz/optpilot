*Explanation of the columns*  

- **¬G** and **¬H** are the negations of G and H.  
- **¬G ⊃ H** is the material conditional “if ¬G then H”. It is false only when the antecedent (¬G) is true **and** the consequent (H) is false; otherwise it is true.  

**Identify the rows where *both* premises are true**

- Premise 1 (¬G ⊃ H) must be **T**.  
- Premise 2 (¬H) must be **T**.

Looking at the table, the only row that satisfies both premises is the second row:

| G | H | ¬G | ¬H | ¬G ⊃ H |
|---|---|----|----|--------|
| **T** | **F** | F | **T** | T |

In that row the conclusion **G** is also **T**.

**Check for a counterexample**

A counterexample would be a row where both premises are true **and** the conclusion is false. No such row exists in the table. Therefore, there is **no** situation in which the premises are true while the conclusion is false.

**Result**

Since every valuation that makes the premises true also makes the conclusion true, the argument is **valid**.
