1. **Identify the key characteristic of the United Kingdom’s constitution.**  
   - The UK does **not** have a single, written constitutional document (i.e., it is *uncodified*).  
   - Its constitutional rules are drawn from several sources: statutes (e.g., the Human Rights Act 1998), common law, conventions, works of authority (e.g., A V Dicey’s *An Introduction to the Study of the Law of the Constitution*), and historical documents (e.g., Magna Carta).

2. **Evaluate each option against that characteristic.**  

   - **(A) “The UK Constitution's only source of power is that of the sovereign.”**  
     - Incorrect. While the doctrine of parliamentary sovereignty is central, the constitution also derives authority from statutes, judicial decisions, conventions, etc.

   - **(B) “The UK Constitution gives the judiciary the power to overturn acts of parliament.”**  
     - Incorrect. Under parliamentary sovereignty, courts cannot strike down primary legislation; they can only interpret it and, in limited circumstances (e.g., compatibility with the European Convention on Human Rights after the Human Rights Act), issue a declaration of incompatibility.

   - **(C) “The UK Constitution is uncodified and can be found in a number of sources.”**  
     - Correct. This precisely captures the uncodified nature and the multiplicity of sources (statutes, common law, conventions, etc.).

   - **(D) “The UK Constitution is based on a Bill of Rights.”**  
     - Incorrect. The UK does have a *Bill of Rights* (1689) and a *Human Rights Act* (1998), but the constitution is not *based* solely on a single Bill of Rights; it is a broader, uncodified framework.

3. **Select the best description.**  
   - Option **(C)** aligns with the accepted scholarly description of the UK constitution.

**Answer:** **(C) The UK Constitution is uncodified and can be found in a number of sources**.
