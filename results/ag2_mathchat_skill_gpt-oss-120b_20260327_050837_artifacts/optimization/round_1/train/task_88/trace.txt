We have to find the smallest integer \(n\) for which there exist distinct positive integers  

\[
s_{1},s_{2},\dots ,s_{n}\;(>1)

satisfying  

\[
\prod_{i=1}^{n}\Bigl(1-\frac1{s_i}\Bigr)=\frac{51}{2010}.
\]

Writing each factor as \(\dfrac{s_i-1}{s_i}\),

\[
\frac{\displaystyle\prod_{i=1}^{n}(s_i-1)}{\displaystyle\prod_{i=1}^{n}s_i}= \frac{51}{2010}
          =\frac{17}{670}\qquad(\text{after dividing numerator and denominator by }3).
\]

Thus we need distinct integers \(s_i\ge 2\) such that  

\[
\boxed{\displaystyle\frac{\prod (s_i-1)}{\prod s_i}= \frac{17}{670}} .
\tag{1}
\]

--------------------------------------------------------------------

### 1.  Reducing the problem to a product of “consecutive‑integer” fractions  

For each \(i\) the two numbers \(s_i\) and \(s_i-1\) are coprime.  
Consequently any prime factor that appears in a denominator \(s_i\) can only be cancelled
by the same prime appearing in a numerator \(s_j-1\) of a *different* term.

Equation (1) tells us that after all possible cancellations the reduced fraction must be  

\[
\frac{17}{2\cdot 5\cdot 67}.
\]

Hence, in the whole collection of numbers \(\{s_i\}\cup\{s_i-1\}\)

* the prime \(17\) must survive **only** in a numerator,
* the primes \(2,5,67\) must survive **only** in denominators,
* every other prime that appears must appear the same number of times in numerators and denominators (so that it cancels).

--------------------------------------------------------------------
