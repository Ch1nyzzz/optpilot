Output: **Step‑by‑step reasoning**

1. **What does IR spectroscopy measure?**  
   Infrared radiation causes vibrational transitions in molecules. A bond will absorb IR light only if the vibration leads to a change in the dipole moment of the molecule.

2. **Do *all* bonds absorb IR radiation?**  
   No. Homonuclear diatomic molecules (e.g., O₂, N₂) have no dipole change during vibration, so they are IR‑inactive. Many C–C bonds in non‑polar environments also give very weak or no absorption. Therefore option (A) is false.

3. **Are IR peak intensities related to molecular mass?**  
   The intensity of an IR band depends primarily on the change in dipole moment during the vibration and on the concentration of the absorbing species, not directly on the molecular mass. Hence option (B) is false.

4. **Do functional groups have characteristic absorption regions?**  
   Yes. Different functional groups (e.g., carbonyl, O–H, N–H, C–H, C≡C, C=C) have vibrational modes that fall in relatively narrow, reproducible frequency ranges (e.g., C=O around 1700 cm⁻¹, O–H stretch broad 3200‑3600 cm⁻¹). This regularity allows chemists to infer the presence of particular groups from an IR spectrum. Thus option (C) is true.

5. **Do individual elements absorb at characteristic wavelengths?**  
   Elements themselves do not have IR absorptions; it is the *bonds* (and the associated functional groups) that produce the bands. Option (D) is therefore false.

**Conclusion**  
The statement that best explains why IR spectroscopy is useful for structural determination is:

**(C) most organic functional groups absorb in a characteristic region of the IR spectrum**.
