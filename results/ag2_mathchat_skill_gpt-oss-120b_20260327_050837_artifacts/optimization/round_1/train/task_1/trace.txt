We are given  

\[
\log _x\!\bigl(y^{\,x}\bigr)=10 ,\qquad 
\log _y\!\bigl(x^{\,4y}\bigr)=10 ,
\qquad x>1,\;y>1 .
\]

Recall that \(\log _a b =c\iff a^{c}=b\).  
Hence the two equations are equivalent to  

\[
\boxed{y^{\,x}=x^{10}}\tag{1}
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\boxed{x^{\,4y}=y^{10}}\tag{2}
\]

---

### 1.  Write the equations with natural logarithms  

Take \(\ln\) of both sides:

\[
\begin{aligned}
x\ln y &= 10\ln x \quad\text{(from (1))},\\[2mm]
4y\ln x &= 10\ln y \quad\text{(from (2))}.
\end{aligned}\tag{3}
\]

These are two linear equations in the unknowns \(\ln x\) and \(\ln y\).

---

### 2.  Eliminate \(\ln y\)

From the first relation of (3),

\[
\ln y = \frac{10\ln x}{x}.
\]

Insert this expression for \(\ln y\) into the second relation of (3):

\[
4y\ln x = 10\left(\frac{10\ln x}{x}\right)
          =\frac{100\ln x}{x}.
\]

Because \(\ln x\neq 0\) ( \(x>1\) ), we may divide by \(\ln x\):

\[
4y = \frac{100}{x}\qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad
y = \frac{25}{x}.
\]

---

### 3.  Find \(xy\)

Multiplying the last equality by \(x\) gives

\[
xy = 25.
\]

Thus the product of the two numbers is uniquely determined, regardless of the individual values of \(x\) and \(y\).

---

### 4.  (Optional) Verify that a solution exists  

Substituting \(y=25/x\) back into the first equation of (3):

\[
x\bigl(\ln 25 - \ln x\bigr)=10\ln x
\;\Longrightarrow\;
x\ln 25 = (x+10)\ln x .
\]

This equation indeed has a positive solution \(x>1\) (numerically \(x\approx 1.534\)), and then \(y=25/x\approx 16.29\). Both satisfy the original logarithmic conditions, confirming that the derived product \(xy=25\) is attainable.
