Output: **Step‑by‑step reasoning**

1. **Define the quantities**  
   - *Actual brightness* (also called **luminosity**, \(L\)) is the total power the object emits in all directions (intrinsic property).  
   - *Apparent brightness* (also called **flux**, \(F\)) is the power per unit area that we receive at our location.

2. **Recall the physical relationship**  
   For an isotropic emitter the flux we measure follows the inverse‑square law:  

   \[
   F = \frac{L}{4\pi d^{2}}
   \]

   where \(d\) is the distance from the source to the observer.

3. **Solve for the unknown**  
   Rearranging the equation to isolate \(d\):

   \[
   d = \sqrt{\frac{L}{4\pi F}}
   \]

   Thus, if we know **both** \(L\) (actual brightness) **and** \(F\) (apparent brightness), we can compute the distance \(d\).

4. **Check the other options**  
   - **Speed relative to you** – requires Doppler shift or proper motion data, not just brightness.  
   - **Composition** – needs spectroscopic information, not brightness alone.  
   - **Size** – could be inferred only if we also know surface brightness or angular size; brightness alone is insufficient.  

   None of these can be uniquely determined from just \(L\) and \(F\).

5. **Conclusion**  
   The only quantity that can be estimated with no additional information is the **distance** to the object.

**Answer:** **(D) Its distance from you**.
