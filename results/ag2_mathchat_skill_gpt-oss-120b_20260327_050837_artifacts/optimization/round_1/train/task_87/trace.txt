Let the rows be numbered \(1,2,\dots ,100\) and the columns \(1,2,\dots ,100\).
A king placed on the square \((r,c)\) attacks every square whose
coordinates differ from \((r,c)\) by at most \(1\) in each direction,
i.e. the eight squares that share a side or a vertex with \((r,c)\).

--------------------------------------------------------------------
### 1.  What does the “no‑capture’’ condition imply?

If two kings are on squares \((r,c)\) and \((r',c')\) then we must have  

\[
|r-r'|\ge 2\qquad\text{or}\qquad |c-c'|\ge 2 .
\]

Consequently a king can be placed only on squares that are **pairwise
at distance at least \(2\) in the Chebyshev metric**.

Consider the residues of the coordinates modulo \(4\).
For any two squares whose residues are equal,
\[
r\equiv r'\pmod 4,\qquad c\equiv c'\pmod 4,
\]
the differences \(|r-r'|\) and \(|c-c'|\) are multiples of \(4\); hence
they are never equal to \(1\).  
Thus **all squares belonging to the same residue class of the pair
\((r,c)\) modulo \(4\) are mutually non‑adjacent**.

Conversely, if two squares belong to different residue classes then
their coordinates differ by \(1\) in at least one direction, so they are
adjacent (they share a side or a vertex).  
Therefore a set of squares satisfying condition (i) must be contained
in a single residue class of the map  

\[
(r,c)\longmapsto (r-c)\pmod 4\qquad\text{or}\qquad (r,c)\longmapsto
(r+c)\pmod 4 .
\]
