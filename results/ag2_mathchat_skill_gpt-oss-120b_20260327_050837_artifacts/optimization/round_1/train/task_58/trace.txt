1. **Identify the nature of the communication**  
   - The taxpayer told the accountant facts and statements while the accountant was preparing a financial statement.  
   - The accountant is *not* the taxpayer’s attorney; he is a third‑party expert hired at the attorney’s suggestion.

2. **Consider the attorney‑client privilege (Option A)**  
   - This privilege protects communications *between a client and his/her lawyer* (and, in some jurisdictions, communications with agents of the lawyer made **for the purpose of obtaining legal advice**).  
   - The accountant was hired to compile financial data, not to give legal advice.  
   - Therefore the communications are **not** covered by the attorney‑client privilege.  
   - **Option A is incorrect.**

3. **Consider the Fifth‑Amendment self‑incrimination privilege (Option B)**  
   - The privilege is personal to the individual who would be compelled to testify.  
   - It does **not** extend to prevent a third party (the accountant) from testifying about statements the taxpayer made to that third party.  
   - Hence the taxpayer cannot block the accountant’s testimony on this ground.  
   - **Option B is incorrect.**

4. **Consider the work‑product doctrine (Option C)**  
   - The work‑product rule shields *documents* and tangible things prepared by an attorney or his/her agents **in anticipation of litigation**.  
   - It does **not** protect the underlying facts or oral testimony about those facts.  
   - The accountant’s testimony about what the taxpayer said is not a protected work‑product document.  
   - **Option C is incorrect.**

5. **Assess whether the testimony is admissible as an admission (Option D)**  
   - A statement made by a party‑opponent (the taxpayer) is admissible as a **non‑hearsay admission** under the Federal Rules of Evidence (Rule 801(d)(2)).  
   - The accountant is merely relaying the taxpayer’s own statements; the testimony is therefore admissible as an admission.
