For a regular \(n\)-gon (\(n=2006\)) label the vertices \(0,1,\dots ,n-1\) clockwise.
For two vertices \(i\) and \(j\) let  

\[
d(i,j)=\min\bigl(|i-j|,\;n-|i-j|\bigr)
\]

be the number of sides on the shorter arc between them.  
A diagonal (or a side) is **odd** iff \(d(i,j)\) is odd.  
Thus an odd diagonal connects vertices whose index difference is odd.

A triangulation of a convex \(n\)-gon uses exactly \(n-3\) interior diagonals and produces
\(n-2\) triangles.

--------------------------------------------------------------------

### 1.  Geometry of the required isosceles triangles  

Because the polygon is regular, two chords have the same length **iff** the
corresponding index differences are equal.  
Consequently an isosceles triangle whose two equal sides are odd must be of the
form  

\[
\triangle\bigl(v,\;v-k,\;v+k\bigr),\qquad k\text{ odd},\;1\le k\le\frac{n}{2}.
\]

* the two equal sides are the odd diagonals \((v,v-k)\) and \((v,v+k)\);
* the third side \((v-k,v+k)\) has length \(2k\) – an even diagonal (or a side
  when \(k=1\), but then the triangle is the “ear’’ \((v-1,v,v+1)\)).

Hence every admissible triangle is either  

* an **ear** \((v-1,v,v+1)\) – it uses two polygon sides (both odd) and no interior
  diagonal, or  

* a **central** triangle \((v,v-k,v+k)\) with \(k\ge 3\) odd – it uses two odd
  interior diagonals and one even interior diagonal.
