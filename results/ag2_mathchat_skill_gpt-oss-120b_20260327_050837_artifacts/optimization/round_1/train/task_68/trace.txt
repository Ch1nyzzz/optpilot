### 1.  What a *tight* sub‑collection looks like  

For a non‑empty sub‑collection \({\mathcal A}\subseteq {\mathcal F}\) write  

\[
U({\mathcal A})=\bigcup_{A\in{\mathcal A}}A ,\qquad 
d_x({\mathcal A})=\bigl|\{A\in{\mathcal A}:x\in A\}\bigr|
\quad(x\in X).
\]

\({\mathcal A}\) is **tight** iff  

* \(U({\mathcal A})\neq X\) (the union is a proper subset of \(X\)), and  
* \(d_x({\mathcal A})\neq 1\) for every \(x\in X\) (each element is either absent
  from the union or belongs to at least two members of \({\mathcal A}\)).

Thus a tight sub‑collection is a family of subsets whose incidence matrix
has **no row of weight 1** and whose column‑sum vector is not the all‑ones
vector.

different 2‑element subsets that involve exactly three points, for example  

\[
\{12,13,23\}.
\]

exactly two of the three sets; the fourth point occurs in none.
Consequently **any family that contains a triangle of 2‑subsets is already
tight**.

* a \(t\)-element set \(T\) and

present in at least two of the three sets, while the points of \(X\setminus
