### Statement 1  
*“A factor (quotient) group of a non‑Abelian group is non‑Abelian.”*

1. **Recall definition** – A factor (quotient) group \(G/N\) is formed from a group \(G\) and a normal subgroup \(N\le G\).  
2. **Check the claim** – The statement says *every* such quotient must be non‑Abelian whenever \(G\) itself is non‑Abelian.  
3. **Search for a counterexample** –  
   *Take \(G=S_{3}\), the symmetric group on three letters.*  
   - \(S_{3}\) is non‑Abelian (e.g. \((12)(13)\neq(13)(12)\)).  
   - Its normal subgroup \(A_{3}=\{e,(123),(132)\}\) has index 2.  
   - The quotient \(S_{3}/A_{3}\) has order 2, hence is isomorphic to \(\mathbb{Z}_{2}\), which is Abelian.  
4. **Conclusion** – A non‑Abelian group can have an Abelian quotient, so the statement is **false**.

---

### Statement 2  
*“If \(K\) is a normal subgroup of \(H\) and \(H\) is a normal subgroup of \(G\), then \(K\) is a normal subgroup of \(G\).”*

1. **Recall normality transitivity** – Normality is **not** transitive in general.  
2. **Construct a concrete counterexample** –  
   - Let \(G=S_{4}\) (order 24).  
   - Let \(H=A_{4}\) (the alternating subgroup, order 12). Since \([G:H]=2\), \(A_{4}\) is normal in \(S_{4}\).  
   - Inside \(A_{4}\) consider the Klein four subgroup  
     \[
     K=V=\{e,(12)(34),(13)(24),(14)(23)\}.
     \]  
     \(V\) is normal in \(A_{4}\) (it is the unique normal Sylow‑2 subgroup of \(A_{4}\)).  
   - However, \(V\) is **not** normal in \(S_{4}\); there are three distinct Klein‑four subgroups in \(S_{4}\) and they are permuted by conjugation.  
3. **Conclusion** – The hypothesis does **not** guarantee that \(K\trianglelefteq G\); the statement is **false**.

---

### Overall answer  

- Statement 1: **False**  
- Statement 2: **False**

Hence the correct option is **(B) False, False**.
