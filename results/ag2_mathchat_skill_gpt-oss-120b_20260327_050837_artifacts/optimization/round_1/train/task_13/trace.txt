For a tetrahedron that has an insphere (a point whose distances to all four faces are equal) the inradius \(r\) satisfies  

\[
V=\frac{1}{3}r\;S\qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad r=\frac{3V}{S},
\]

where  

* \(V\) – volume of the tetrahedron,  
* \(S\) – total area of its four faces.

The given tetrahedron is a **disphenoid** (opposite edges are equal):
\[
AB=CD=\sqrt{41},\qquad AC=BD=\sqrt{80},\qquad AD=BC=\sqrt{89}.
\]

A disphenoid is always tangential, i.e. it possesses an insphere, so the formula above can be used.

All four faces are congruent triangles with side lengths  
\(\sqrt{41},\sqrt{80},\sqrt{89}\).  
Hence  

\[
S = 4\;\Delta,\qquad 
\Delta = \text{area of one face}.
\]

We compute \(\Delta\) with Heron’s formula and the volume \(V\) with the known
formula for a disphenoid whose three mutually‑adjacent edges are
\(p,q,r\) (each appears twice):

\[
V = \frac{1}{4}\sqrt{(p^{2}+q^{2}-r^{2})(p^{2}-q^{2}+r^{2})(-p^{2}+q^{2}+r^{2})}.
\]

Finally \(r=3V/S\) is simplified to the required form \(\dfrac{m\sqrt n}{p}\).
