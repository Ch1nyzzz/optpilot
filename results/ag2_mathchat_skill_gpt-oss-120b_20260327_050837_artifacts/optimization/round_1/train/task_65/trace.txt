### 1.  Why a larger value cannot be guaranteed  

Place the \(4n\) points in a regular rectangular grid  

\[
\Bigl\{\bigl(\tfrac{i}{2\sqrt n},\tfrac{j}{2\sqrt n}\bigr)\;:\;
i,j=1,2,\dots ,2\sqrt n\Bigr\},
\qquad (\sqrt{4n}=2\sqrt n)
\]

(the points are inside the open unit square because the grid lines are
strictly between \(0\) and \(1\)).  
The unit square is divided into \(4n\) congruent cells, each cell being an
open rectangle of area  

\[
\frac{1}{4n}.
\]

Two neighbouring points lie on the same vertical or horizontal line that
bounds a cell, therefore any axis‑parallel open rectangle that contains
exactly one of the points cannot extend beyond the four sides of the cell
that contains that point; otherwise it would also contain a neighbour.
Consequently every such rectangle has area at most \(\frac1{4n}\).

Hence no constant larger than \(\frac1{4n}\) can work for **all**
\(4n\)-point configurations, i.e.

\[
\mu_{\max}\le \frac1{4n}.
\tag{1}
\]
