There is **no largest integer** \(k\).  
For every positive integer \(k\) we can split the set of positive integers into
\(k\) disjoint subsets  

\[
\mathbb N = A_{1}\cup A_{2}\cup\cdots\cup A_{k},
\qquad A_{i}\cap A_{j}= \varnothing\;(i\neq j)
\]

so that each \(A_{i}\) has the required property:

> for every integer \(n\ge 15\) there are two *different* elements of \(A_{i}\) whose sum equals \(n\).

Consequently the “greatest” such \(k\) does not exist; the property can be
realised for arbitrarily large \(k\).

--------------------------------------------------------------------

### 1.  Why a positive density is enough  

Let \(B\subset\mathbb N\) be a set whose **lower asymptotic density**

\[
\underline d(B)=\liminf_{N\to\infty}\frac{|B\cap\{1,\dots ,N\}|}{N}
\]

is positive.  
A classical theorem of Erdős–Fuchs (later refined by many authors) says that
if \(\underline d(B)>0\) then the sum‑set  

\[
B+B=\{x+y\;:\;x,y\in B,\;x\neq y\}
\]

contains **all sufficiently large integers**.  
In other words, a set of positive lower density is an *asymptotic basis of
order 2* (the “distinct‑elements’’ condition can be satisfied for large
\(n\) because a representation \(n=2a\) can be replaced by
\(n=(a-1)+(a+1)\) – both numbers belong to \(B\) when the density is
positive).

Hence, if we manage to give each of the \(k\) parts a positive lower
density, each part will automatically be a basis for all integers
\(n\ge 15\) (the finitely many integers below the “sufficiently large’’
threshold can be taken care of by a small, finite adjustment that does not
destroy the partition).
