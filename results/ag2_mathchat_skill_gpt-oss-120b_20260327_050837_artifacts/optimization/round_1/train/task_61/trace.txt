Output: **Step‑by‑step reasoning**

1. **Identify the nature of the claim**  
   The hypothesis “there is an attractive force between all objects” is a universal claim about the behavior of matter. In the philosophy of science (Popperian falsificationism), scientific theories are *never* proven true in an absolute sense; they can only survive attempts at falsification.

2. **Examine each answer choice**  

   - **(A) “The first time you drop a bowling ball …”** – A single observation can at best *support* a hypothesis; it does not constitute proof, because a single instance could be coincidental or explained by other known forces (e.g., gravity).  

   - **(B) “After you’ve repeated your experiment many times.”** – Repetition strengthens confidence (increases the *corroboration* of the hypothesis) but still does not amount to logical proof; future evidence could still contradict it.  

   - **(C) “You can never prove your theory to be correct only ‘yet to be proven wrong’.”** – This reflects the core principle of falsifiability: a scientific theory can never be proven definitively; it can only be *corroborated* until a counter‑example is found.  

   - **(D) “When you and many others have tested the hypothesis.”** – Broad testing again adds corroboration but does not convert the hypothesis into a proven fact; the possibility of future falsification remains.

3. **Select the answer that aligns with scientific methodology**  
   The only option that correctly captures the epistemic status of scientific theories—*they can never be proven, only not yet falsified*—is **(C)**.

**Answer:** **(C) You can never prove your theory to be correct only “yet to be proven wrong”.**
