That is, at most \(\lceil m/2\rceil\) dominoes can be placed in each of the \(n\) column‑pairs, and this bound can always be attained.

--------------------------------------------------------------------

### 1.  Why no column‑pair can contain more than \(\lceil m/2\rceil\) dominoes  

The board consists of \(n\) *column‑pairs*  

\[
C_k=\{(r,2k-1),(r,2k)\},\qquad k=1,\dots ,n,\; r=1,\dots ,m .
\]

A domino that uses the cells of a column‑pair is necessarily **horizontal**  
(the two cells are side‑by‑side).  
If two horizontal dominoes lie in **consecutive rows** of the same column‑pair,
they together form a \(2\times 2\) square, which is forbidden by condition (iii).

Hence, looking at a fixed column‑pair \(C_k\),

* among any two consecutive rows at most one of them can contain a domino of
  \(C_k\);
* consequently the rows that may contain a domino of \(C_k\) must be spaced
  at least one row apart.

From a set of \(m\) rows we can therefore select at most  

\[
\Big\lceil\frac{m}{2}\Big\rceil
\]

rows that are pairwise non‑consecutive.  
Thus

\[
\#\{\text{dominoes using }C_k\}\le \Big\lceil\frac{m}{2}\Big\rceil .
\tag{1}
\]

Because the bottom row (row \(m\)) has to be completely covered, each column‑pair
contains **at least one** domino, so the bound (1) is the exact maximum for a
single column‑pair.

Summing (1) over the \(n\) column‑pairs gives the universal upper bound  

\[
D(m,n)\le n\Big\lceil\frac{m}{2}\Big\rceil .
\tag{2}
\]
