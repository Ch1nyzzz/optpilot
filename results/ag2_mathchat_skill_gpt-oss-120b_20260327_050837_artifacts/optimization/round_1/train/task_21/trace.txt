We need the number of ordered integer pairs \((x,y)\) with  

\[
-100\le x\le 100,\qquad -100\le y\le 100,

that satisfy  

\[
12x^{2}-xy-6y^{2}=0. \tag{1}

---

### 1.  Solve the quadratic equation  

Treat (1) as a quadratic in \(x\):

\[
12x^{2}-yx-6y^{2}=0 .
\]

The discriminant is  

\[
\Delta = y^{2}+4\cdot12\cdot6\,y^{2}=y^{2}+288y^{2}=289y^{2}=(17y)^{2}.
\]

Since \(\Delta\) is a perfect square for every integer \(y\), the two roots are

\[
x=\frac{y\pm 17y}{2\cdot12}
   =\frac{y(1\pm 17)}{24}.
\]

Hence the only possible relationships between \(x\) and \(y\) are  

\[
\boxed{x=\frac34\,y}\qquad\text{or}\qquad\boxed{x=-\frac23\,y}. \tag{2}
\]

---

### 2.  Impose integrality  

* **First family** \(x=\frac34 y\).  
  For \(x\) to be integer, \(y\) must be a multiple of \(4\).  
  Write \(y=4k\) with \(k\in\mathbb Z\). Then \(x=3k\).

  Bounds: \(-100\le 4k\le 100\;\Longrightarrow\;-25\le k\le 25\).  
  Thus \(k\) has \(25-(-25)+1 = 51\) possible values.

  Every such \(k\) gives a pair \((x,y)=(3k,4k)\) that lies inside the \([-100,100]\) square.

* **Second family** \(x=-\frac23 y\).  
  Here \(y\) must be a multiple of \(3\).  
  Write \(y=3m\) with \(m\in\mathbb Z\). Then \(x=-2m\).

  Bounds: \(-100\le 3m\le 100\;\Longrightarrow\;-33\le m\le 33\).  
  Hence \(m\) has \(33-(-33)+1 = 67\) possible values.

  Each gives a pair \((x,y)=(-2m,3m)\) inside the allowed region.

---

### 3.  Remove double‑counted pair  

The two families intersect only when both formulas give the same \((x,y)\).  
Setting \(\frac34 y = -\frac23 y\) yields \(17y=0\Rightarrow y=0\), and then \(x=0\).  

Thus the pair \((0,0)\) appears in both families.  

Total distinct ordered pairs:

\[
51\;(\text{first family})\;+\;67\;(\text{second family})\;-\;1\;(\text{overlap}) = 117.
\]
