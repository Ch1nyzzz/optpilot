### Why two squares are impossible  

If  

\[
x^{2}+7 = f_{1}(x)^{2}+f_{2}(x)^{2},
\qquad f_{i}\in\mathbb Q[x],
\]

then each \(f_{i}\) must be at most linear, because the degree of the right–hand side is
\(2\max\{\deg f_{1},\deg f_{2}\}\).  
Write  

\[
f_{1}(x)=a_{1}x+b_{1},\qquad 
f_{2}(x)=a_{2}x+b_{2},
\qquad a_{i},b_{i}\in\mathbb Q .
\]

Comparing coefficients gives  

\[
\begin{cases}
a_{1}^{2}+a_{2}^{2}=1,\\[2pt]
2(a_{1}b_{1}+a_{2}b_{2})=0,\\[2pt]
b_{1}^{2}+b_{2}^{2}=7 .
\end{cases}
\tag{1}
\]

The first two equations have rational solutions (they describe a rational point on the
unit circle), but the last one asks for a rational point on the circle
\(b_{1}^{2}+b_{2}^{2}=7\).  
The Diophantine equation  

\[
p^{2}+q^{2}=7r^{2}
\]

has only the trivial solution \(p=q=r=0\): reducing modulo \(4\) forces
\(r\) to be even, then \(p,q\) are even, and an infinite descent shows that no
non‑zero integer solution exists. Consequently the circle \(b_{1}^{2}+b_{2}^{2}=7\)
has **no rational point**, and (1) cannot be satisfied.  
Hence a representation with two squares is impossible.

--------------------------------------------------------------------
