A path from the lower‑left corner \((0,0)\) to the upper‑right corner \((8,8)\) on an \(8\times8\) grid must consist of exactly  

* 8 steps to the right (R) and  
* 8 steps upward (U),

so every admissible path is a word of length \(16\) containing eight \(R\)’s and eight \(U\)’s.

A *direction change* occurs whenever two consecutive steps are different (…R U… or …U R…).  
If a path has \(k\) maximal blocks of identical steps (called **runs**), then it has exactly \(k-1\) direction changes.

The problem asks for paths that change direction **exactly four times**, i.e. paths that have  

\[
k-1 = 4 \;\Longrightarrow\; k = 5
\]

runs.  
Thus each desired path is a sequence of **five alternating runs** of R’s and U’s, beginning either with an R‑run or with a U‑run.

--------------------------------------------------------------------

### 1.  Counting paths that start with **R**

The pattern of runs is  

\[
\underbrace{R}_{\text{run 1}}\;\underbrace{U}_{\text{run 2}}\;\underbrace{R}_{\text{run 3}}\;\underbrace{U}_{\text{run 4}}\;\underbrace{R}_{\text{run 5}}
\]

Hence we need  

* three positive integers \(r_1,r_2,r_3\) (the lengths of the three R‑runs) with  
  \(r_1+r_2+r_3 = 8\);
* two positive integers \(u_1,u_2\) (the lengths of the two U‑runs) with  
  \(u_1+u_2 = 8\).

The number of ways to split an integer \(n\) into \(k\) **ordered** positive parts (compositions) is  

\[
\binom{n-1}{k-1}.
\]

Therefore  

* ways to choose the three R‑run lengths: \(\displaystyle \binom{8-1}{3-1}= \binom{7}{2}=21\);
* ways to choose the two U‑run lengths: \(\displaystyle \binom{8-1}{2-1}= \binom{7}{1}=7\).

Multiplying gives the number of paths that start with R:

\[
21 \times 7 = 147.
\]
