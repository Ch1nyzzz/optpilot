For a board of size \(m\times m\) the last ant must fall off no later than  

\[
\boxed{\,m^{2}\,}
\]

seconds after the start, and this bound is best possible.  
Consequently, for a fixed \(m\) a finite “latest moment’’ exists; it is
\(m^{2}\) seconds, and for every larger time one can exhibit a starting
configuration that forces the last ant to fall **after** that time.

--------------------------------------------------------------------

### 1.  Why an ant can never survive longer than \(m^{2}\) seconds  

All ants start at the centres of the unit squares, i.e. at points  

\[
\Bigl(i+\tfrac12,\;j+\tfrac12\Bigr),\qquad 0\le i,j\le m-1 .
\]

Hence every ant’s \(x\)– and \(y\)–coordinates are always half‑integers
between \(\tfrac12\) and \(m-\tfrac12\).

When an ant meets another ant that is moving in the opposite direction
it turns **clockwise** by \(90^{\circ}\).  
Consequently the direction of an ant can only change in the cyclic
order  

\[
\text{up}\;\longrightarrow\;\text{right}\;\longrightarrow\;
\text{down}\;\longrightarrow\;\text{left}\;\longrightarrow\;\text{up}
\tag{1}
\]

and never reverses (a reversal would be a \(180^{\circ}\) turn, which
never occurs).  
Thus every ant can experience at most three direction changes before it
starts moving again in a direction it has already used.
