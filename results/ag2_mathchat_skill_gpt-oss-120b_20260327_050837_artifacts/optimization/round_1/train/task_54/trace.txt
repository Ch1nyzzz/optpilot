Output: **Step‑by‑step reasoning**

1. **What is the “leverage effect”?**  
   In financial time‑series it refers to the empirical observation that *negative* returns (price drops) tend to increase future volatility more than *positive* returns of the same absolute size. This asymmetry is often attributed to the fact that a fall in a firm’s equity value raises its debt‑to‑equity ratio, making the equity riskier (hence “leverage”).

2. **What is a “news‑impact curve”?**  
   In GARCH‑type volatility models the news‑impact curve plots the conditional variance (or volatility) **after** a shock as a function of the size of that shock (the standardized residual). For a symmetric model (e.g., standard GARCH) the curve is symmetric around zero: a shock of +x and –x produce the same variance increase.

3. **How does a model that *accounts* for leverage modify the curve?**  
   To capture the leverage effect, the model must allow the impact of a shock to depend on its sign. Typical specifications (e.g., EGARCH, GJR‑GARCH, TGARCH) add a term that is multiplied by an indicator for negative shocks. Consequently, for a given absolute magnitude |ε|, the conditional variance after a **negative** shock is larger than after a **positive** shock.

4. **Translating this to the shape of the curve:**  
   - For **negative disturbances** the curve rises *more steeply* because the extra leverage term adds to the variance.  
   - For **positive disturbances** the curve follows the baseline (symmetric) part of the model and therefore rises more slowly.

eq1 = sp.Eq(L, 2*W)          # Length is twice the width

W_rounded = round(float(W_val), 2)
