We want all positive integers \(n\) such that  

\[
n+2 \;\big|\; 3\,(n+3)(n^{2}+9).
\]

---

### 1.  Replace \(n+2\) by a new variable  

Let  

\[
m=n+2\qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad n=m-2,
\]

so \(m\) is a positive integer and, because \(n\ge 1\), we have \(m\ge 3\).

The divisibility condition becomes  

\[
m \;\big|\; 3\big((m-2)+3\big)\big((m-2)^{2}+9\big)
      = 3(m+1)\big((m-2)^{2}+9\big).
\]

Compute the quadratic term:

\[
(m-2)^{2}+9 = (m^{2}-4m+4)+9 = m^{2}-4m+13 .
\]

Hence we need  

\[
m \mid 3(m+1)(m^{2}-4m+13).
\]

---

### 2.  Reduce the expression modulo \(m\)

When a number is divided by \(m\), we may replace each factor by its remainder modulo \(m\):

\[
\begin{aligned}
m+1 &\equiv 1 \pmod m,\\
m^{2}-4m+13 &\equiv 13 \pmod m \qquad(\text{since } m^{2}\equiv 0,\; -4m\equiv 0).
\end{aligned}
\]

Therefore  

\[
3(m+1)(m^{2}-4m+13) \equiv 3\cdot 1 \cdot 13 = 39 \pmod m .
\]

Thus the original divisibility condition is equivalent to  

\[
m \mid 39 .
\]

---

### 3.  Find admissible \(m\)

The positive divisors of \(39\) are  

\[
1,\; 3,\; 13,\; 39 .
\]

Because \(m=n+2\ge 3\), we discard \(m=1\).  
Hence  

\[
m\in\{3,13,39\}.
\]

---

### 4.  Return to \(n\)

\[
n = m-2 \;\Longrightarrow\;
\begin{cases}
m=3  &\Rightarrow n=1,\\[2pt]
m=13 &\Rightarrow n=11,\\[2pt]
m=39 &\Rightarrow n=37.
\end{cases}
\]

All three values satisfy the original condition (a quick substitution confirms it).

---

### 5.  Sum of all such \(n\)

\[
1+11+37 = 49 .
\]

\[
