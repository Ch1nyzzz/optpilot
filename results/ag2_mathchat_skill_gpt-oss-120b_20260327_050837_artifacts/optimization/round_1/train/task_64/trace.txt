--------------------------------------------------------------------

### 1.  Why at least five moves are needed  

After Sergey has made \(m\) moves, each index \(k\in\{1,\dots ,20\}\) is either

* **present** in the queried set \(S_i\) of the \(i\)-th move, or  
* **absent** from it.

Hence every index obtains a binary “code’’ of length \(m\)

\[
c(k)=\bigl(\mathbf 1_{k\in S_1},\mathbf 1_{k\in S_2},\dots ,\mathbf 1_{k\in S_m}\bigr)\in\{0,1\}^m .
\]

If two different indices \(p\neq q\) receive the same code, then the whole
sequence of answers that Sergey gets is unchanged when the two numbers
\(a_p\) and \(a_q\) are interchanged (the numbers are distinct, but the
answers are only *sets*).  
Consequently Sergey cannot tell which of the two numbers belongs to
\(p\) and which to \(q\).

Now suppose the codes of \(p\) and \(q\) coincide.  
Choose any admissible family \((a_1,\dots ,a_{20})\) for a certain
\(N\).  
Replace it by the family in which the two values are swapped:
\[
\tilde a_k=
\begin{cases}
a_q & k=p,\\[2pt]
a_p & k=q,\\[2pt]
a_k & \text{otherwise}.
\end{cases}
\]
Both families satisfy the congruence condition
\(a_k\equiv N\pmod k\) (because the congruence depends only on the
index, not on the value), they are still distinct, and they give exactly
the same answers to all \(m\) queries.  
Therefore Sergey cannot distinguish the two possibilities for the pair
\((p,q)\) and consequently cannot determine the residues
\(N\bmod p\) and \(N\bmod q\).  
Since the moduli are different, the lack of these two residues may leave
more than one integer \(N\le 5000\) compatible with the information he
has gathered.

Thus the codes of the 20 indices must be *pairwise different*.  
With \(m\) bits we can create at most \(2^{m}\) different codes, so we need  

\[
2^{m}\ge 20\qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad m\ge\lceil\log_2 20\rceil =5 .
\]

Hence **at least five moves are required**.

### 1.  What does Sergey actually learn?

After `m` queries `S_1 , … , S_m` each index `k` obtains a binary
vector  

```
v(k) = ( 1_{k∈S_1} , 1_{k∈S_2} , … , 1_{k∈S_m} )   ∈ {0,1}^m .
```

When a value `a_k` shows up in the answers, Sergey can read its vector:
it is present exactly in those queries whose answer contained it.
Consequently after the `m` moves Sergey knows the **pairings**

```
{ ( a_k , v(k) ) : k = 1,…,20 } .
```

If two different indices have the same vector, Sergey cannot tell which
of the two corresponding numbers belongs to which modulus, i.e. he does
not know the residues `N (mod k)` for those two indices.

Hence a *necessary* condition for being able to recover `N` is

> the 20 vectors `v(1),…,v(20)` must be pairwise different.

--------------------------------------------------------------------

### 2.  Lower bound – at least 5 moves

With `m` moves there are at most `2^m` different binary vectors.
To have 20 distinct vectors we need  

```
2^m ≥ 20   ⇒   m ≥ ⌈log2 20⌉ = 5 .
```

So **5 moves are necessary**; with 4 moves at most 16 different patterns are
possible, therefore at least two indices share a pattern.
Because the numbers `a_k` are arbitrary (only required to be distinct and
congruent to `N`), Xenia can choose them so that swapping the two
indistinguishable indices changes the value of `N` while leaving all
answers unchanged.  
Thus with 4 moves Sergey cannot guarantee to determine `N`.
