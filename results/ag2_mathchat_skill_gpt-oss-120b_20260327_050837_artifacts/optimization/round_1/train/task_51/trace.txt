### Step‑by‑Step Reasoning

1. **Observe the Phenomenon**  
   - When we look up during daylight, the sky is predominantly a pale‑blue color, except near sunrise/sunset when it turns red/orange.

2. **Identify the Physical Process Involved**  
   - Sunlight is a mixture of all visible wavelengths (roughly 400 nm – 700 nm).  
   - As sunlight passes through the atmosphere, it interacts with gas molecules (N₂, O₂, etc.) and tiny particles.

3. **Recall Rayleigh Scattering Theory**  
   - Rayleigh scattering describes the elastic scattering of light by particles much smaller than the wavelength of light.  
   - The scattering intensity \(I\) varies inversely with the fourth power of wavelength:  
     \[
     I \propto \frac{1}{\lambda^{4}}
     \]
   - Consequently, **short‑wavelength light (blue, ~450 nm) is scattered about 10 times more efficiently than long‑wavelength red light (~650 nm).**

4. **Apply the Theory to the Sky’s Color**  
   - The scattered blue light is redirected in many directions, filling the sky and reaching our eyes from every line of sight.  
   - Redder wavelengths are scattered far less, so they continue mostly straight to the ground, which is why the Sun itself looks white (a mixture) and why the horizon can appear reddish at low solar angles (the path through the atmosphere is longer, allowing more scattering of blue and leaving red).
