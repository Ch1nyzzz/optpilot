For a fixed set of \(2n\) points on a circle (labelled \(1,2,\dots ,2n\) clockwise) we consider  

* a *non‑crossing perfect matching* – a partition of the points into \(n\) unordered pairs such that the chords do not intersect, and  
* an orientation of every chord (clockwise or counter‑clockwise).

A configuration is **good** iff it contains **no two disjoint chords that are both oriented clockwise**.  
Indeed, if two chords are disjoint their endpoints appear on the circle as  
\(A\;B\;C\;D\) (clockwise) and the forbidden pattern is exactly the pair of arrows
\(\overrightarrow{AB},\overrightarrow{CD}\).

Hence the set of clockwise‑oriented chords must be *nested*: any two of them must
one contain the other. In the language of the chord‑poset (partial order by
inclusion) the clockwise chords form a **chain**.

Consequently, for a given non‑crossing matching \(M\) the admissible
orientations are in one‑to‑one correspondence with the chains of the
inclusion‑poset of its chords (the empty chain corresponds to the orientation
where every chord points counter‑clockwise).

Thus the required number equals  

\[
\boxed{\displaystyle
\sum_{M\in\mathcal{NC}_n} \bigl(\text{number of chains in the chord‑poset of }M\bigr)},
\]

where \(\mathcal{NC}_n\) denotes the set of non‑crossing perfect matchings on
\(2n\) points.
