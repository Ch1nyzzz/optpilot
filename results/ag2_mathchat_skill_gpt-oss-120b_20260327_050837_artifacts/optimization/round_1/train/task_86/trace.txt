### 1.  What a “diverse’’ set means  

A flag is a binary word of length \(N\); we write a yellow field as \(0\) and a blue
field as \(1\).

Let  

\[
F=\{f_{1},f_{2},\dots ,f_{k}\}\qquad (f_{i}\in\{0,1\}^{N})
\]

be a collection of distinct flags.
Choose a colour \(c\in\{0,1\}\) and a permutation \(\sigma\) of \(\{1,\dots ,N\}\).
If for every \(i\)

\[
f_{\sigma(i)}[i]=c ,
\]

then the \(N\) flags \(\{f_{\sigma(1)},\dots ,f_{\sigma(N)}\}\) can be placed as the
rows of an \(N\times N\) square whose main diagonal is monochromatic (all
\(c\)).  
Thus a set of \(N\) flags is **diverse** iff there is a colour \(c\) for which the
family  

\[
\mathcal A_c=\bigl\{\,\{j\mid f_i[j]=c\}\;:\;i=1,\dots ,N\bigr\}
\]

has a system of distinct representatives (SDR); i.e. a perfect matching
between the \(N\) flags and the \(N\) positions using only the positions that
contain the colour \(c\).

Consequently we have to guarantee a perfect matching in at least one of the
two bipartite graphs

\[
G_0\;( \text{edges }f_i\!-\!j\text{ when }f_i[j]=0),\qquad   
G_1\;( \text{edges }f_i\!-\!j\text{ when }f_i[j]=1).
\]
