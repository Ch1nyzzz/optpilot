For a given base \(b>9\) we interpret the “numbers” written in that base in the usual way:

\[
17_{b}=1\cdot b+7 = b+7,\qquad 
97_{b}=9\cdot b+7 = 9b+7 .
\]

We need all integer bases \(b>9\) for which \(17_{b}\) divides \(97_{b}\); i.e.

\[
b+7 \mid 9b+7 .
\]

---

### 1. Reduce the divisibility condition  

Let  

\[
d = b+7 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad b = d-7 .
\]

Substituting \(b = d-7\) into the numerator:

\[
9b+7 = 9(d-7)+7 = 9d - 63 + 7 = 9d - 56 .
\]

Thus the condition becomes  

\[
d \mid 9d-56 .
\]

Since \(d\) obviously divides \(9d\), the above is equivalent to  

\[
d \mid 56 .
\]

So **\(d\) must be a positive divisor of \(56\).**

---

### 2. Apply the base‑size restriction  

We are told \(b>9\). Because \(d = b+7\),

\[
d = b+7 > 9+7 = 16 .
\]

Hence we need divisors of \(56\) that are **greater than 16**.

Divisors of \(56\) are  

\[
1,\;2,\;4,\;7,\;8,\;14,\;28,\;56 .
\]

The ones exceeding \(16\) are \(28\) and \(56\).

---

### 3. Recover the corresponding bases  

\[
\begin{aligned}
d = 28 &\Longrightarrow b = d-7 = 21,\\[2mm]
d = 56 &\Longrightarrow b = d-7 = 49 .
\end{aligned}
\]

Both bases satisfy the original digit‑validity condition (the digits \(1,7,9\) are all less than the base because \(b>9\)).

A quick check:

* For \(b=21\): \(17_{21}=28,\; 97_{21}=196,\; 196/28 = 7\) (integer).  
* For \(b=49\): \(17_{49}=56,\; 97_{49}=448,\; 448/56 = 8\) (integer).

Thus they are indeed valid.

---

### 4. Sum of all such bases  

\[
21 + 49 = 70 .
\]

---
